# Chapter 14: The Death Penalty Cases: — More of the Same

**I**n my previous books, I wrote about my philosophical and juridical opposition to the Death Penalty Law.  Thus, in *Battles in the Supreme Court,*1 I discussed quite extensively the Supreme Court “battle” over the constitutionality of RA 7659, insofar as it reimposed the death penalty; and of RA 8177, which prescribed lethal injection as the method of carrying out the capital punishment.

In *Leadership by Example,*2 I detailed the significant jurisprudential developments involving death cases.  And in *Transparency, Unanimity **&** Diversity,*3 I reported *inter alia* on then President Joseph E. Estrada’s declaration of an “11-month moratorium on the executions of all convicts whose capital sentences had been affirmed by the Supreme Court x x x out of respect for the worldwide celebration of the Jubilee

Since that presidential declaration which was reported by the news dailies on March 25, 2000, there had indeed been no executions, because the President granted reprieves and commutations.

This no-execution policy was continued by the new government of President Gloria Macapagal Arroyo.  First announced by Justice Secretary Hernando Perez on March 7, 2001,4 the policy was reiterated by then Executive Secretary Renato de Villa on April 7, 2001.  The latter was quoted as saying that “there will be no executions of death row convicts during [President Arroyo’s] three-year term x x x.”5

Secretary de Villa explained in a radio interview that “there are no executions because the President automatically commutes death sentences when the sentences are already going to be carried out, or before they are carried.”

Indeed, to this date, no one has been placed in the lethal injection chamber since the Estrada announcement on March 25, 2000.  In the meantime, to repeal the death penalty, several legislators have filed bills in Congress — notably Sen. Aquilino Pimentel Jr. and Sen. John Osmeña in the Senate; and Rep. Krisel Lagman-Luistro and Rep. Emilio Macias II in the House of Representatives.  Inasmuch as the law has not been repealed, the Supreme Court has continued reviewing death sentences imposed by trial courts.

*Jurisprudential Developments*

In spite of a long line of decisions reiterating that the attendant circumstances mentioned in Section 11 of RA 7659 are in the nature of qualifying circumstances and must be *both* alleged in the information *and* proven during the trial, there were still many cases in which the Regional Trial Courts imposed the capital penalty without observing this doctrine.  Because of these lapses committed by both the prosecution and the trial court, a vast majority of the decisions of the Supreme Court have imposed lesser penalties.  I will no longer report on each of these many cases.  However, I will just point out some salient jurisprudential developments on the death penalty, from October 11, 2000 to October 10, 2001, my sixth year in the Court.

1.  The failure of the prosecution to allege the “use of deadly weapon” in a rape by means of force and intimidation entitles the accused to the lower penalty of *reclusion perpetua.*6

2.  Exemplary or corrective damages may be imposed when one or more aggravating circumstances, whether qualifying or ordinary, are proven.  This remains true, even if the offense was committed prior to the effectivity of the new Rules on Criminal Procedure requiring that such “qualifying or aggravating circumstances” must be alleged in the information.  According to the Court, the retroactive application of procedural rules cannot adversely affect the offended party’s *civil* rights, which had already vested prior to the effectivity of the rules. Justice Jose C. Vitug, the Court declared in *People v. Catubig:*7

“The term ‘aggravating circumstances’ used by the Civil Code, the law not having specified otherwise, is to be understood in its broad or generic sense.  The commission of an offense has a two-pronged effect, one on the public as it breaches the social order and the other upon private victim as it causes personal sufferings, each of which is addressed by, respectively, the prescription of heavier punishment for the accused and by an award of additional damages to the victim.  The increase of the penalty or a shift to a graver felony underscores the exacerbation of the offense by the attendance of aggravating circumstances, whether ordinary or qualifying, in its commission.  Unlike the criminal liability which is basically a State concern, the award of damages, however, is likewise, if not primarily, intended for the offended party who suffers thereby.  It would make little sense for an award of exemplary damages to be due the private offended party when the aggravating circumstance is ordinary but to be withheld when it is qualifying.  Withal, the ordinary or qualifying nature of an aggravating circumstance is a distinction that should only be of consequence to the criminal, rather than to the civil, liability of the offender. or qualifying, should entitle the offended party to an award of exemplary damages within the unbridled meaning of Article 2230 of the Civil Code.

“Relevantly, the Revised Rules on Criminal Procedure, made effective on 01 December 2000, requires aggravating circumstances, whether ordinary or qualifying, to be stated in the complaint or information.  Sections 8 and 9 of Rule 110 of the Rules of Court now provide:

*New Cases on Improvident Plea*

15.  In *People v. Aranzado,*29 the Court through Chief Justice Hilario G. Davide Jr. bemoaned the trial court’s failure to conform to the exacting standards of the accused’s constitutional right to due process, as well as the lackluster performance of the counsel of the accused who had pleaded guilty.  Of special guidance in these cases is the strict observance of Section 3 of Rule 116 of the Rules of Court, which reads as follows:

“Sec. 3.  *Plea of guilty to capital offense; reception of evidence.*  – When the accused pleads guilty to a capital offense, the court shall conduct a searching inquiry into the voluntariness and full comprehension of the consequences of his plea and shall require the

prosecution to prove his guilt and the precise degree of culpability.  The accused may present evidence in his behalf.”

Accordingly, when the accused pleads guilty in a case involving a capital offense, the trial court must rigidly observe the following guidelines:

“1.  The court must conduct a searching inquiry into the voluntariness and full comprehension by the accused of the consequences of his plea;

“2.  The court must require the prosecution to present evidence to prove the guilt of the accused and precise degree of his culpability; and

“3.  The court must ask the accused if he desires to present evidence in his behalf and allow him to do so if he desires.”

Because of the significance of this ruling, even if it echoes earlier pronouncements, I shall quote liberally from the Chief’s *ponencia* as follows:

“On the first requirement, it bears to note that a searching inquiry must focus on the voluntariness of the plea and the full comprehension of the consequences of the plea30 so that the plea of guilty can be truly said to be based on a free and informed judgment. “In *McGautha vs. California,*67 the US Supreme Court noted that there was almost from the beginning, a ‘rebellion against the common-law rule imposing a mandatory death sentence on all convicted murderers.’  In response, State legislatures resorted to restricting the death penalty to defined offenses such as premeditated murder or divided the culpability for the crime of murder into degrees, with the mandatory death penalty imposable only for murder in the first degree. “North Carolina also made similar attempts to remedy the statutory defects identified in *Furman* by swinging to the extreme opposite direction.  It totally eliminated jury discretion through mandatory death penalty laws.  By holding as unconstitutional the provision of its death penalty statute that gave the jury unbridled discretion to return a verdict of guilty of first-degree murder without capital punishment, and by holding further that such provision was severable, the North Carolina statute survived as a mandatory death penalty law.  Its legislature then enacted a new statute making the death penalty mandatory for first-degree murder, which includes any willful, deliberate and premeditated killing and any murder committed in perpetrating or attempting to perpetrate a felony.  In *Woodson vs. North Carolina,*71 the US Supreme Court struck down North Carolina’s mandatory death penalty as unconstitutional.  *Woodson* pronounced that North Carolina’s mandatory scheme, instead of rationalizing the problem, might well exacerbate the problem identified in *Furman* by resting the penalty determination on a particular jury’s

willingness to act lawlessly.  North Carolina had misread *Furman.*  It was not the presence of jury discretion that made death penalty cruel and unusual but the arbitrary, irrational, excessive and discriminatory quality of its imposition.  The *Woodson* Court, in dismissing the mandatory death penalty statute as unconstitutional, belabored the statute’s exclusion from the sentencing process, the relevant facets of the character and record of the individual offender or circumstances which could be peculiar to every offense.
