# Chapter 19: Bengson v. House of Representatives Electoral Tribunal: — Repatriation Restores Original Citizenship

**R**epatriation is a mode of reacquiring Philippine citizenship as provided under Republic Act No. 2630.  What is reacquired in repatriation?  Is it the “original” citizenship that was previously lost, or is it an entirely “new” one?  In the present case, private respondent lost his *natural-born* citizenship when he served in the US armed services and was naturalized as a US national.  By his repatriation, did he reacquire his *natural-born* Philippine citizenship?

The Court en banc answered this question in the affirmative in *Bengson v. House of Representatives Electoral Tribunal.*1

*The Facts*

Teodoro C. Cruz was born of Filipino parents on April 27, 1960 in San Clemente, Tarlac.  Thus, under

1  GR No. 142840, May 7, 2001.

the 1935 Constitution,2 he was a natural-born citizen.  On November 5, 1985, however, he enlisted in the US Marine Corps, took an oath of allegiance to the United States, and thus lost his Philippine citizenship as a consequence.  Under Section 1(4) of Commonwealth Act No. 63, citizenship may be lost by, among others, “rendering service to or accepting commission in the armed forces of another country.”  On June 5, 1990, he was naturalized as an American.

On March 17, 1994, Cruz was repatriated under Republic Act No. 2630.  During the 1998 national elections, he was elected representative of the Second District of Pangasinan, with a convincing margin of 26,671 over Antonio E. Bengson III, who was then running for reelection.

Dismissing a Petition for *Quo Warranto* filed by Bengson, the House of Representatives Electoral Tribunal (HRET) declared Cruz as fully qualified to sit in the House of Representatives.  Bengson elevated the matter to the Supreme Court via a Petition for Certiorari, charging the HRET with grave abuse of discretion in ruling that Cruz had reacquired his original (natural-born) citizenship.

Justice Angelina Sandoval-Gutierrez, to whom the case had originally been assigned, recommended the grant of the Bengson Petition.  But during the deliberation, Justice Kapunan and I filed separate Dissents.  By majority vote,3 the Dissents were upheld.  I deferred to Justice Kapunan, who was the more senior member of the Court; thus, he wrote the *ponencia,* while my Dissent was converted to a Concurring Opinion.  In turn, the erstwhile Gutierrez draft became the Dissenting Opinion.

*The Ruling*

In junking the Petition, Justice Kapunan explained the two ways of acquiring citizenship: (1) by birth and (2) by naturalization.  These correspond to two kinds of citizens: the natural-born and the naturalized.

Natural-born citizens, according to the Constitution, are “those who are citizens from birth without having to perform any act to acquire or perfect such Philippine citizenship”; while naturalized citizens are “those who have become citizens through naturalization, generally under Commonwealth Act No. 473 and Republic Act No. 530.”  On the other hand, citizenship may be reacquired by a former citizen under Commonwealth Act No. 63 (1) by naturalization, (2) by repatriation and (3) by a direct act of Congress.  Since he did not reacquire his Philippine nationality by naturalization or by a direct act of Congress, then Cruz must be natural-born.

Debunking petitioner’s contention that Cruz could not be considered natural-born because he had to perform an act to regain his citizenship, Justice Kapunan wrote:

“x x x.  As correctly explained by the HRET in its decision, the term ‘natural-born citizen’ was first defined in Article III, Section 4 of the 1973 Constitution, as follows:

‘Sec. 4.  A natural-born citizen is one who is a citizen of the Philippines from birth without having to perform any act to acquire or perfect his Philippine citizenship.’

“Two requisites must concur for a person to be considered as such: (1) a person must be a Filipino citizen from birth and (2) he does not have to perform any act to obtain or perfect his Philippine citizenship.

“Under the 1973 Constitution definition, there were two categories of Filipino citizens which were not considered natural-born: (1) those who were naturalized and (2) those born before January 17, 1973,4 of Filipino mothers who, upon reaching the age of majority, elected Philippine citizenship.  Those ‘naturalized citizens’ were not considered natural-born obviously because they were not Filipinos at birth and had to perform an act to acquire Philippine citizenship.  Those born of Filipino mothers before the effectivity of the 1973 Constitution

were likewise not considered natural-born because they also had to perform an act to perfect their Philippine citizenship.

“The present Constitution, however, now considers those born of Filipino mothers before the effectivity of the 1973 Constitution and who elected Philippine citizenship upon reaching the majority age as natural-born.  After defining who are natural-born citizens, Section 2 of Article IV adds a sentence: ‘Those who elect Philippine citizenship in accordance with paragraph (3), Section 1 hereof, shall be deemed natural-born citizens.’  Consequently, only naturalized Filipinos are considered not natural-born citizens.  It is apparent from the enumeration of who are citizens under the present Constitution that there are only two classes of citizens: (1) those who are natural-born and (2) those who are naturalized in accordance with law.  A citizen who is not a naturalized Filipino, i.e., did not have to undergo the process of naturalization to obtain Philippine citizenship, necessarily is a natural-born Filipino.  Noteworthy is the absence in said enumeration of a separate category for persons who, after losing Philippine citizenship, subsequently reacquire it.  The reason therefor is clear: as to such persons, they would either be natural-born or naturalized depending on the reasons for the loss of their citizenship and the mode prescribed by the applicable law for the reacquisition thereof.  As respondent Cruz was not required by law to go through

*My Concurring Opinion*

My Concurring Opinion listed five reasons why the Petition should be dismissed, and why Cruz should be deemed a natural-born Filipino citizen, as follows.

*“1.  Repatriation Is Recovery*

*of Original Citizenship*

“*First,* repatriation is simply the recovery of *original* citizenship.  Under Section 1 of RA 2630, a person ‘who ha[s] lost his citizenship’ may ‘reacquire’ it by ‘taking an oath of allegiance to the Republic of the Philippines.’  Former Senate President Jovito R. Salonga, a noted authority on the subject, explains this method more precisely in his treatise *Private International Law.*5  He defines repatriation as ‘the recovery of the *original* nationality upon fulfillment of certain conditions.’6  Webster buttresses this definition by describing the ordinary or common usage of *repatriate* as ‘to restore or return to one’s country of origin, allegiance, or citizenship; x x x.’7  In relation to our subject matter, *repatriation* then means restoration of citizenship.  It is *not* a grant of

a *new* citizenship, but a recovery of one’s former or original citizenship.

“To ‘reacquire’ simply means ‘to get back as one’s own again.’8  *Ergo,* since Cruz, prior to his becoming a US citizen, was a natural-born Filipino citizen, he ‘reacquired’ the same status upon repatriation.  To rule otherwise – that Cruz became a nonnatural-born citizen – would not be consistent with the legal and ordinary meaning of repatriation.  It would be akin to naturalization, which is the acquisition of a new citizenship.  ‘New,’ because it is not the same as that with which he has previously been endowed.

“In any case, ‘the leaning, in questions of citizenship, should always be in favor of [its] claimant x x x.’9  Accordingly, the same should be construed in favor of private respondent, who claims to be a natural-born citizen.

*“2.  Not Being Naturalized,*

*Respondent Is Natural Born*

“*Second,* under the present Constitution, private respondent should be deemed natural-born, because he was not naturalized.  Let me explain.

*The Dissent*

Justice Angelina Sandoval-Gutierrez’s Dissent traced the historical evolution of our law on citizenship and opined that “the status of being a natural born citizen at its incipient is a privilege conferred by law directly to those who intended, and actually continued, to belong to the Philippine Islands.  Even at the time of its conception in the Philippines, such persons upon whom citizenship was conferred did not have to do anything to acquire full citizenship”

Since Cruz had to perform certain acts, like taking an oath of allegiance to the Republic of the Philippines, registering his oath with the Local Civil Registry, renouncing his American citizenship and executing an

affidavit of reacquisition, he did not reacquire his natural-born citizenship.

The constitutional definition of a natural-born citizen must be applied according to its natural sense.  She concluded:

“For sure, the framers of our Constitution intended to provide a more stringent citizenship requirement for higher elective offices, including that of the office of a Congressman.  Otherwise, the Constitution should have simply provided that a candidate for such position can be merely a citizen of the Philippines, as required of local elective officers.

“The spirit of nationalism pervading the 1935 Constitution, the first charter framed and ratified by the Filipinos (even as the draft had to be approved by President Franklin Delano Roosevelt of the United States) guides and governs the interpretation of Philippine citizenship and the more narrow and bounded concept of being a natural-born citizen.

**A Salute to My Mentor**

**I** would like to begin by calling attention to and elucidating my own knowledge of an incident mentioned on page 75 of the book being launched today, *A Journey of Struggle and Hope.*

I was a freshman at the Far Eastern University Institute of Law in 1956, the school year barely a few weeks old, when suddenly the law students, who were a thousand strong, declared a “strike” in protest of what they perceived was the precipitate and unjustified retirement of the well-loved and venerable dean of the law school.  As a new law student, I did not personally know our old dean, but I went along with the strikers as they filled the FEU auditorium with chants, slogans and anguished cries of “Persecution!”

Then, a youngish 36-year-old bespectacled professor, a Yale and Harvard graduate and bar topnotcher, walked to center stage, approached the microphone, and singlehandedly faced the hostile

**Appendix B**

**Justice Is God’s Work**

**A Prelude to Paperless Courts**

**T**he third millennium and the twenty-first century ushered in new paradigms, technologies and biosciences.  As a macro consequence of these paradigms, the struggle for world preeminence has shifted from a battle of ideologies and weapons of mass destruction to one of trade dominance.  In the economic and political fields, the strategy has shifted from government control to deregulation, from state ownership to privatization, from national sovereignty to globalization, and from protectionism to trade liberalization.

On the other hand, new computer technologies have accelerated both the obsolescence of the industrial era and the advent of the Information Age.  Indeed, the possession and the distribution of information through instant, lightning-quick electronic data transmission, via the Internet and fiber optics, have become as valuable as oil resources, gold mines, heavy industries and agricultural products.

*  Opening Remarks of Justice Artemio V. Panganiban during the Centenary Lecture entitled “Paperless Courts: The Australian Experience,” delivered by Justice Robert Nicholson of the Federal Court of Australia on September 26, 2001 in the Session Hall of the Philippine Supreme Court.

Meanwhile, the emerging field of biosciences has made it possible to clone living organisms and to replicate or reproduce human organs through the extra-womb development of stem cells.  DNA testing, genetic engineering and other new scientific discoveries promise healthier and longer lives even if, at the same time, they raise ethical and moral concerns.  Given these breakthroughs, our children and grandchildren can look forward to much longer life spans of 150 or even 200 years.

These massive economic, political, technological and scientific developments are challenging and transforming not only the geographic and demographic face of the world, but also everything else that matters — political systems, lifestyles, work habits, philosophies, religious beliefs, leisure times, family lives and, yes, even the normally reclusive and exclusive judicial enclaves.

This afternoon, as a consequence also of these new technologies and sciences, we are journeying into one specific transformational shift in our legal realm — the elimination of paper.  At first blush, this proposition seems imponderable, if not totally impossible and absurd.  How can paper be eliminated from our profession?  Indeed, the law is said to be the most paper-dependent discipline in the world.  In no other field is the advice “Put it in writing” more prevalent than in the law.  Whenever a statute or a contract states that something “shall be in writing,” the unsaid assumption is that it shall be written on paper.  So, too, our present procedural rules like those on parol evidence, statute of frauds, best evidence, and many others show a definite bias in favor of documentation.

APPENDICES

And whenever the law speaks of documentation, we always assume that it refers to something written on paper.  While the Ten Commandments may have been engraved in stone and Confucian wisdom in wooden tablets, until recently we have always thought of paper as the medium of written communication.

In line with the passage on June 11, 2000, of Republic Act No. 8792 (otherwise known as the Electronic Commerce Act or ECA), the Supreme Court has recently promulgated the Philippine Rules on Electronic Evidence, which took effect less than two months ago on August 1, 2001.

Together, the ECA and the new Rules on Electronic Evidence eliminate the “disparity of treatment between data messages and paper documents.”  Also, an electronic document is regarded as the functional equivalent of a paper-based document; and an electronic or a digital signature, the functional equivalent of a traditionally penned “John Hancock.”  In brief, the new Rules we have just adopted makes it possible to admit in evidence electronically produced documentation or data messages.

The Supreme Court feels honored and proud of being one of the first tribunals in Asia to produce these Rules and, thereby, to keep in step with the electronic age.  Let me, however, stress that there is as yet no intention in the said law and Rules to eliminate paper as the primary medium of communication in the bar or the bench.

Today, during the lecture and the open forum that will follow, we shall assess whether paper can indeed be expunged from legal practice, and whether

paperless courts are possible in our country.  We are singularly honored to have with us as centenary lecturer and resource speaker the Honorable Justice Robert Nicholson of the Federal Court of Australia, who will discuss the Australian experience in this bold new venture.  It is with this mixed feeling of anticipation, awe and anxiety that I welcome all of you today to what may historically be the epochal prelude to paperless legal proceedings in our country.

Thank you and good day.

*of*

*Justice Artemio V. Panganiban’s*

**A CENTENARY OF JUSTICE**

*Justice Artemio V. Panganiban’s*

**A CENTENARY OF JUSTICE**

*Supreme Court Printing Press*

*December 2001*

*413 pages*

*A Book Review by ***Justice HILARION L. AQUINO**

*Court of Appeals*

In the repertoire of a concert artist, there is usually a *piece de resistance,* and all that comes before builds towards its sterling rendition as a powerful climax.  Like a concert artist, Justice Artemio V. Panganiban has rendered one masterpiece after another: his *ponencias* and separate opinions are examples of artfully written and forcefully argued judicial literature, and his books — of which this is the fifth — dissect, with clinical accuracy, often tangled legal problems while affording the reader the pleasure of unpretentious prose and a warm glimpse at the High Court’s seldom-seen side!  Unlike a concert artist, however, Justice Art does not keep in reserve his juristic acumen and his literary artistry for a *piece de resistance,* because everything he offers either as a magistrate or a writer (and it is not easy to distinguish when he is one or the other) is a masterpiece, not really because he is set on creating an impression, but because he shares of himself without reserve in all that he writes.  What one then has from him is always a piece of his soul and a privileged view of his spirit — and that is always worth any reader’s while!

In *Old Doctrines and New Paradigms,* a paper he read for the Centenary Lecture Series of the Supreme Court, the author selects decisions of the Court which, to him, break new ground insofar as they rest on new paradigms.  He announces, for example, that “there is an unmistakable shift from the state to the private sector.”  This is a persuasion shared by contemporary social analysts, and the French philosopher Francois Lyotard is among them.  The center of gravity, many are persuaded, will no longer be the state but individuals and corporations.  This of course provokes the question about what the role of the State will be even as the inexorable trend appears to be towards “decentralization, deregulation.”  Some have gone so far as to suggest that State interference will be like “static” that garbles communication, a thesis Justice Art obviously will not share.  When one reads further on in his disquisition however that the collapse of totalitarianism and the bankruptcy of collectivist societies have led to greater protection of individual rights and liberties, then it should not be too difficult to infer that the author suggests that the proper role of State power will be the protection and enhancement of individuals’ capacity to agree among themselves and to decide.

The author is unabashed about admitting his biases.  Bias is one more concept that contemporary thought has vindicated.  In the past, one had to apologize for his biases.  Today, it is common teaching that all knowledge is possible because of that mass of background information and conviction we call bias.  Among the biases of Justice Panganiban is his consistent position against the death penalty, a position we already know of from his separate and dissenting opinions in the death penalty cases.  In fact, the author devotes an entire chapter to the issue, Chapter 14; and while he underscores such considerations as the insouciance of the defense counsel, if not of the judge who ought to proceed in such cases with great circumspection, he also rightly invokes the strength of growing global opposition to the death penalty.

With candor, but without being offensive, Justice Art likewise takes issue with what he considers the erosion of the protection due laborers, when the Court maintained that the requirement of due process is a limitation on state power but not on private action.  One can understand that the author would like labor better protected, but not a few will take issue with him on this point, for there is good reason to distinguish — as recent jurisprudence has done — between those limitations the fundamental law institutes against state power and those that it sets up against the actions of private individuals.

The advance of the sciences in the genome project and in DNA analysis must be exciting to lawyers and jurists.  One will not fail to catch the refrain of Justice Art’s position: The judge cannot be a spectator to the progress in other human disciplines with nonchalance and a noncommittal posture.  Already, scholars have asked whether or not the Philippine judiciary is prepared to cope with such questions as relate to the presentation of DNA test results and matching procedures.  The author suggests the course of the future: “By its rule-making power, the Supreme Court can promulgate rules on DNA fingerprinting, the use of genes as evidence, and the admission of biotechnology and genetics as evidence.”  While, of course, it is always possible for the Supreme Court to settle such issues when it hears and decides cases involving them, it can take a more pro-active stance by issuing rules providing for admissibility or exclusion, and thus anticipate what could be very difficult questions left to the devices of the individual judge.

One very charming piece in the book is “E-Values for Lawyers.”  Even if he was addressing lawyers, Justice Art started by echoing the humbling but telling observation that more are more cynical about the legal profession.  Lawyers will not welcome this broadside from laypersons, but from a member of the highest court of the land, it was a necessary wake-up call.  He proposes his three values: Excellence, Ethics and Eternity.  E-values, for an E-age.  There is nothing “e” about them, except their first letters, because they do not depend on intricate and sometimes unfriendly e-technology, but they are enduring values even, and maybe particularly, for an e-age.  The legal profession has a noble lineage and a venerable ancestry.  It should not be indifferent to public cynicism and distrust, and the task of redeeming the profession falls squarely on the shoulders of lawyers themselves.  Justice Art’s e-values may help them discern the work that needs to be done.

While neither the Chief Justice nor the author took part in the deliberations of the Court leading to its doctrinally innovative decisions in *Estrada v. Macapagal-Arroyo,* Justice Art explains the reason for his recusal or inhibition (taking care, at the same time, to distinguish between *disqualification and inhibition or recusal*).  He makes clear that the petitioner had failed to make a case to disqualify him from participating in the proceedings, even as he insists that all such motions must be firmly anchored in law.  However, he thought it the more prudent course to inhibit himself so that there would be no doubt at all on the integrity of the proceedings and on the fairness of whatever decision the Court would render.  No justice would have relished the prospect of being excluded from the crafting of a decision that would surely be studied by generations of law students and jurists to come, and of leaving one’s mark in the form of a concurring or a dissenting opinion.  The personal decision Justice Art had to make, and which he recounts, calls attention to that dilemma that will, on various occasions, confront a judge: while his voluntary inhibition will be the safer and the more comfortable option — as it will often provide him with the excuse to keep as far away as possible from a tinderbox — a too frequent recourse to the expedient of a voluntary inhibition may in fact be a form of dereliction of one’s duty!

Everywhere in the world, the claim of indigenous peoples to land occupied and cultivated since time immemorial has provoked very interesting and trying legal issues, has engendered novel jurisprudence and spurred the emergence of ingenious legislation.  Canada and Australia are examples of jurisdictions that have evolved a body of substantive and procedural law to deal with the problem of what is often called “native title.”  It was then with bated breath that the Philippine legal community awaited the Supreme Court’s verdict on the constitutional challenge to the validity of the Indigenous Peoples’ Rights Act (IPRA), and many shared the disappointment of petitioner — Mr. Justice Isagani Cruz — when, because the court was evenly split, the petition was dismissed.  The author discusses the legal theories invoked by his colleagues and explains his own position that was adverse to the law.  In sum, the author’s objection to the law was that considerable portions of Philippine territory would be effectively placed outside the reach of the Philippine Constitution and of the State power it established were one to maintain the theory of the law’s advocates that these were never in fact lands of the public domain.  In fact, what concerned the author was that the claim covered — and potentially included — huge tracts of land.  This is an issue that blurs the already blurred line between law and policy.  Orthodox teaching has it that justiciability goes only so far as law, and that policy is left to the Legislature.  The fact, however, is that when a judge adjudicates due and demandable rights, the law is applied according to its policy.  And when the

validity of a law is impugned, consistency with national policy as this is reflected in the fundamental law of the land will not be an irrelevancy.

What makes for engaging reading is literature that engages the reader in the struggles, pains and disappointments of the author’s soul.  This is a quality one will not find wanting in the author’s book.  In discussing the decision of the Court in *Perez v. Estrada* that reiterated the prohibition against live television coverage of the criminal trials of the erstwhile President, Justice Art could barely conceal his profound disappointment with the majority position when he wrote: “And yet, the Court, by its decision today, has turned its back and refused to march in cadence with the music created by the chimes of changing technology.”  He sided very strongly with the proponents of live coverage in the name of transparency and accountability.  Unfortunately for Justice Art, he was opting for the reversal of a well-entrenched and long-established tradition, not only in the Philippines but elsewhere.  It might not be amiss to add that a more generous allowance demands maturity on their part.  In large measure, the reticence of the Court about media coverage was also a statement of its wariness about the sensationalism and, yes, irresponsibility that have unfortunately marred media’s image.

True to his cherished belief, the author endeavors to keep the public informed, for it can only be helpful to the Supreme Court — and to the judiciary as a whole — for the public to be educated about its constitutionally apportioned task as well as its role in a democratic state.

The piece on the Supreme Court Justice Panganiban renders for us in *A Centenary of Justice* is one that gives us its *pianissimos* and its *fortissimos,* in tempo that is at times *allegro,* at other times *adagio.*  He invites respect and esteem for the Court on the occasion of its centenary, not because of any pretension at infallibility, but because of its consecration to the often painful and contentious struggle for that measure of justice under the law that is *supremely* available to us who must labor under the vagaries of changing times, circumstances and shifting opinions and philosophical persuasions.

And even as we applaud yet one more masterpiece from this consummate artist of juridical and legal literature, we wait with undiminished excitement for his next rendition!  *Abangan!*
