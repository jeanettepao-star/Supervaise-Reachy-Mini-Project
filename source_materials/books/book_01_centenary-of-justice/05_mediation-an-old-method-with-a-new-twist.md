# Chapter 5: Mediation, an Old Method  with a New Twist

*[**One of the major problems of the judiciary is its clogged dockets.  It seems that Filipinos are a litigious people.  The number of cases filed are more than those decided by the courts.  The net result is delayed justice.  Thus, the Supreme Court has proposed various reforms to address this basic complaint of our people.  These solutions were essayed in Chapters 1 and 2 of Justice Panganiban’s earlier book, *Transparency, Unanimity & Diversity* (2000 ed., pp. 3-45).  One solution mentioned was to encourage our people to avail themselves of alternative dispute resolution (ADR) methods, especially mediation.  The Supreme Court’s policy on mediation is explained in this chapter, which is taken from Justice Panganiban’s speech during the “Advocates’ Forum for Media” held on March 16, 2001, at the Linden Suites, Ortigas Business District, Pasig City.]*

**D**uring the ancient days, men settled their disputes by subduing their opponents with the use of superior muscles, swords or guns.  The stronger person, clan, tribe or nation prevailed and dominated the weaker ones.  Sheer force decided conflicts.  Might dictated right.

Nowadays, there are still those who settle their conflicts in similar ways; in fact, people have crafted more sophisticated weapons of destruction and invented subtler modes of coercion — short of actual force — like bribery, blackmail and even sorcery.

But gentility and civilization have taught us that in the long term, right is better than might; the pen, more powerful than the sword; and reason, more reliable than aggression.  Hence, humankind created constitutions, codes and courts to spell out more civilized ways of governing, regulating, acting and settling disputes.

In our country, we believe in our Constitution.  From grade school, we are taught that our individual rights and liberties are inviolable.  Laws and ordinances have been enacted to regulate relationships and interactions.  And to resolve conflicts and vindicate rights peacefully and civilly, courts have been created.  Litigation has become the standard method of resolving disputes.

To be sure, however, litigation has many disadvantages — it is tedious, time-consuming, stressful, expensive, and most often, ends up in broken relationships and permanent animosities among the parties, even after a final judgment has been rendered.  Instead of healing controversies, judgments sometimes inflict deeper wounds and engender vengeance as well as new cycles of violence.

The e-age trend is to avoid litigation and to find alternative solutions to resolve disputes — solutions that are less time-consuming, less tedious, less confrontational and more productive of goodwill and lasting relationships.

Ever inventive, human beings have crafted several substitutes to litigation.  The most popular of these are negotiation, conciliation, mediation and arbitration.  These are collectively referred to as ADRs — Alternative Dispute Resolution methods.

ADRs are by no means novel.  Some of them are even older than litigation itself.  But lately, they have been refined and polished to become more effective and acceptable methods of settling controversies.  The present effort to promote their use in our country is indeed new and fresh.  In brief, they are old methods with a new twist.

Verily, they form an integral part of the judicial reform program of the Supreme Court.  During its centenary celebrations, the Court would like to popularize them and promote their use.  We believe that the proper and extensive recourse to these alternative methods would not only decongest court dockets, but also result in better and more civilized solutions to human disputes.

Rule 18 of the Rules of Court requires trial courts to consider, during the pretrial stage, the possibility of submitting a controversy to ADRs.  The very new Interim Rules on Corporate Rehabilitation (Rule 4) also impels such referral as a mandatory step during a pretrial conference.  Even the new Electronic Commerce Act and the Retail Trade Liberalization Law encourage the use of ADRs.

In a very recent case, *Purefoods Corporation v. Court of Appeals* (GR No. 128066, June 19, 2000), the High Tribunal grieved that the “simple case” between the parties “could have been resolved through mediation and conciliation during its infancy stage, had the parties been earnest in expediting the disposal of this case.  They opted, however, to resort to full court proceedings and denied themselves the benefits of alternative dispute resolution, thus making the process arduous and long drawn.”  The dispute involved the purchase and the installation of generators to alleviate the effects of the power crisis in 1992.  The case was finally decided only in the year 2000, when the crisis was already over and the generators no longer needed by the buyer.

Of the ADRs I mentioned, mediation is what we are focusing on today.  Armed with a grant from the Asia Foundation, the Supreme Court and its education arm, the Philippine Judicial Academy, have conducted several workshops and seminars on this topic.

To direct and conduct the Court’s training program for mediators and mediation advocates, the Court and its co-sponsors are proud to have retained the services of Mrs. Annabelle T. Abaya, a Boston-based Filipino mediator.  She has prepared herself in this field, not only with graduate studies at the University of Massachusetts and Harvard, but also with success stories in her handling of actual cases.  She has proven the value of mediation in solving various potential litigations ranging from small family problems to mind-boggling international disputes.

Mediation, as an alternative method of settling legal controversies, is encouraged by the Supreme Court.  We know that, more than litigation or

arbitration, it will bring solutions to disputes speedily, conveniently and peacefully.  Being much less confrontational, mediation conforms with the Asian and Filipino ways of solving problems without loss of face to the claimants.  More significantly, because it is conducted tactfully and diplomatically, it can even improve relationships and interactions between parties after the problem is solved.  Mediation is especially important in cases of continuing relationships like family disagreements, labor and tenancy problems especially in special economic and freeport zones, commercial disputes, consumer controversies and *barangay* level disagreements.

As will be explained at greater length later on in this program by Ms Abaya and Dean Eduardo D. de los Angeles, mediation will henceforth become part of our ordinary vocabulary and will be as popular as “your Honor,” “objection sustained,” “irrelevant and immaterial” – phrases which the public learned during the Senate impeachment trial.  Its effectivity and timeliness in resolving conflicts will be shown ten days from now during our celebration of “Settlement Week” from March 26 to April 6.

During this period, over 300 regional trial courts in Metro Manila, Cebu and Davao will simultaneously refer actual court cases to 300 trained mediators.  The latter will demonstrate from a few hours to a maximum of a few days that mediation, properly used, can resolve conflicts speedily, conveniently and peacefully.

Indeed, mediation resolves controversies.  Beyond that, it ushers in better harmony and improved relationships in the future.  Hence, its promotion and more extensive use is fully encouraged by the Supreme
