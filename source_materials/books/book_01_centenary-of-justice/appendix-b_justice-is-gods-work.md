# Appendix B: Justice Is God’s Work

**T**en days ago, I received an invitation from the Philippine Judicial Academy (Philja) to deliver the closing address today to end formally the Second Pre-Judicature Program.  I would like you to know that a Philja invitation partakes of the nature of a summons, which cannot be refused or ignored not only on pain of default but, worse, of contempt.

It is said that Supreme Court justices are a courageous lot.  They have braved the onslaughts of lynch mobs, screaming headlines and even presidential tyrants and dictators.  Let me, however, tell you that if there is *anyone* over whom high magistrates sweat bullets, that singular one is Justice Ameurfina Aguinaldo Melencio Herrera, the venerable chancellor of Philja.  Not that she is fearsome, but that she is respected and loved.  When she calls, we answer; when she invites, we consider it a command and we

Let me add that the summons for today’s meeting was personally served on me, not by an ordinary sheriff, but by my esteemed former colleague in the Supreme Court, Justice Antonio M. Martinez.  Let me just recall a bit of what I said of Justice Tony on the occasion of his retirement from the Court on February 1, 1999:

“Regrettably, Mr. Justice Martinez has had a stint in the Court that was too brief.  We his colleagues shall miss him and his cadenced responses to the challenges that faced us.  We shall pine for his scholarly *ponencias,* his industry and his passion for truth and fairness.  His stint may have been too brief, but he has certainly enriched jurisprudence in our land.  He has definitely left his unmistakable imprint in my memory of great jurists.”

What has made the invitation even more compelling is the presence of the chairman of the Pre-Judicature Program, Justice Ricardo C. Puno.  He is held in high regard in the Court and is invariably invited to appear as *amicus curiae* whenever the High Tribunal is faced with novel and difficult questions — invitations he has never turned down, even if he has never been paid for his appearance.

Let me begin by congratulating all of you, dear graduates.  You are the first batch to finish this Pre-Judicature Program in the *first* year of the *first* century of the *new* millennium, a distinction that happens only once every one thousand years!  Today, indeed, marks the opening of a major door to the exclusive and

reclusive world of the men and women in black robes.  As you all know, the Philja Pre-Judicature Program is now a prerequisite to a Judicial and Bar Council recommendation for membership in the Philippine judiciary.

As I contemplated my message to you today, memories of my own entry into the judiciary, direct to the Supreme Court in 1995, streamed into my mind.  As I began to brush shoulders with the most exclusive brethren in the country, I was filled not only with awe and anxiety at what lay ahead, but also with some intellectual and spiritual soul-searching.

Let me explain that from 1986 to 1995, my wife and I underwent a spiritual rebirth as we joined the *Bukas Loob sa Diyos,* a charismatic community of renewed Catholics.  During those nine years, we embarked on a new journey of faith through a marriage encounter, a “Life in the Spirit” seminar, retreats and various other programs to relearn and reexperience our faith.  We read and reread the Bible several times over, discussed volumes — nay, libraries — of commentaries thereon.  Later on we shared our experiences by speaking in seminars and conferences, and we even became servant-leaders of this community.

In the process, we had a most fulfilling encounter with our Lord Jesus Christ.  We can truly claim that we came to know Him and to experience His love in many ways.

On my part, I have learned to trust Him and to depend on Him for *all* my needs, aspirations and wants.  Many times I would pray.  Even if I do not get what I want, still, I continue to repose my full faith and

Let me repeat: “God makes *all* things work for the good of those who love Him and who are called according to His purpose.”

The most basic of our Lord’s teachings is love.  When He was asked which commandment of the law was the greatest, He replied, “The first is: love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul and with all your strength.  The second is: Love your neighbor as yourself.”1

1  Mt 22:34-40; Mk 121:28-31.

On another occasion, our Lord was preaching on a mountainside, and among other things, he said, “My command to you is: love your enemies, pray for your persecutors.  This will prove that you are sons of your heavenly Father, for His sun rises on the bad and the good.  He rains on the just and the unjust.  If you love only those who love you, what merit is there in that?  Do not tax collectors do as much?  And if you greet your brothers only, what is so praiseworthy about that?  Do not pagans do as much?  In a word, you must be made perfect as your heavenly Father is perfect.”2

In the face of this core teaching of our Lord Jesus Christ, I asked myself several times: “If God is the very personification of love, why does He punish His people?  At the very least, why does He allow calamity, cruelty, poverty and affliction to assault and damn them?  Why does He not just satisfy all of their needs and wants, something that is definitely within His infinite power and authority?  Why does He not just eradicate poverty, hunger and pain?  He is God; He certainly can do all that.”

It is with this background of incisive theological questions that, upon my appointment to the Supreme Court, I asked myself: “Our Lord’s greatest commandment is to love God and His people; why am I being called to impose penalties for crimes, enforce difficult obligations and sanction burdensome laws?”  I was baffled by the seeming contradiction between love and punishment, between affection and affliction, until I came upon this little story.

A demure young lady was brought before a traffic court for over-speeding.  In the courtroom, the judge

asked her: “Young lady, this police officer tells me that you were racing at 100 kilometers per hour in a 50-kilometer zone.  What can you say about that?”

“He’s correct, your Honor.  I am truly sorry I broke the law, but I promise not to do it again,” replied the repentant miss.

“Because of your confession and promise to reform, the law will be lenient on you.  I will just fine you P300 instead of the usual P500,” the judge solemnly decreed.

After adjourning the court session, the judge removed his black robe, went straight to the cashier, pulled out his wallet and paid the P300 fine.  He paid the penalty out of love, because the lawbreaker was his daughter.  *Justice* was served — and so was *love.*

In a larger and real sense, the judge in our little story is our Lord Jesus Christ.  And the young lady represents each one of us, all of us in this hall.  His Honor is our Divine Judge and we are His unworthy children.  We, His people, sinned.  We violated the law of God.  And so the justice of God requires our punishment.

Because justice is in the nature of God, He — even if He is the most powerful Being in the universe — cannot refuse to impose the sanction of His own law.  Otherwise, He would be violating His own nature.

But Jesus is also love personified.  So, out of His great love, He became man, came down to earth, suffered, was crucified, died and gloriously resurrected.  He atoned for our sins and owned the punishment meant for us.  He did this because of His great love for us.

2  Mt 5:44-48.

Let me stress it again.  Just as our Lord is a God of love, He is also a God of justice.  Just as it is in His nature to love, it is also in His essence to see that justice is done.  Just as He cannot refuse to love, neither can He decline to render justice.  Both love and justice are in His very nature and essence.  He cannot contradict himself by refusing to fulfill both love and justice.

By sending His only Son and by His Son’s suffering, death and resurrection, He saved humankind from eternal damnation.  In this manner, His justice and His love were both fulfilled.

Ladies and gentlemen, if my fifteen-minute talk today is worth anything, it is this: the rendition of justice is not only a human necessity; it is a divine essentiality.  When we give people their due, impose penalties for crimes, uphold liberty, enforce obligations and compel obedience to duties — fairly and speedily, without fear or favor — we really do the work of the Lord.  In short, to dispense *quality* justice is to do God’s work.

To all the graduates, welcome to the divine work of rendering quality *justice* to our fellowmen and to the world!

I thank you.
