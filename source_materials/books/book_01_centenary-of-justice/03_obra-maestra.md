# Chapter 3: Obra Maestra

*[**One of the most enduring centenary projects of the Court was the publication of the *Centenary Reader* consisting of three volumes entitled (1) *Supreme Court Decisions as Literature*, (2) *Supreme Court Decisions as Philosophy*, **and (3) *Supreme Court Decisions as History.*  This trilogy contains the most quotable excerpts from the best decisions of the Court during the last 100 years.  While judicial pronouncements are intended mainly to settle litigations, these three volumes demonstrate that they are also sources of literature, philosophy and history.  Because they constitute selections from the best judicial writings of the highest magistrates of the land, Justice Panganiban calls them **obra maestra** in the Prologue he wrote for the anthology.  This Prologue is reproduced in the following pages.]*

**E**ssentially, the Supreme Court has a twofold responsibility: *first,* to review lower court decisions and correct any “reversible errors”; and *second,* in appropriate cases, to pass upon the acts of *any* branch or instrumentality of government in order to determine whether these have been done with grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction.1

The first constitutes the traditional duty of the judiciary:  to settle actual controversies involving the enforcement of rights, the vindication of wrongs, and the regulation of relationships among the people as well as between individuals and the government.

The second refers to the High Court’s more sensitive, if sometimes controversial, role.  On the one hand, the doctrine of separation of powers, upon which our system of government hinges, decrees that its three great branches — the executive, the legislative and the judicial — should be independent and separate; each one is supreme in matters falling within its own constitutionally allocated sphere.  Thus, no department can or should interfere with the exercise of the rights and the powers of the other two.

On the other hand, when one branch of government abuses the exercise of its powers or crosses its constitutional limits, what remedy may be availed of to vindicate the abuse or excess?  Equally as important, which agency or mechanism would be imbued with the power and the authority to correct the

The United States Supreme Court, from which we borrowed much of our constitutional wisdom, has long answered this question by placing that responsibility upon itself.  During the first few years of its illustrious history, it voided a law enacted in contravention of constitutional provisions.  It thereby “interfered” in the exercise of the lawmaking prerogative, even in the absence of any *express* constitutional authority to do so.

In the Philippines, this American theory of the supremacy of the Constitution, with the Supreme Court as its guardian, has solidified into a granite doctrine.  Thus, whenever the Constitution is violated by a political branch of government — be it by the Presidency or by Congress — the Supreme Court shall, upon proper petition, come in to strike down the transgression.  It shall do so, not because it has any superior-inferior relationship with the violator, but because the Constitution must be upheld as truly paramount.

The doctrine of separation of powers as a stumbling block to the judicial review of governmental actions has been refined further into the “political question” theory.  Such proposition bars courts from looking into matters that, under the Constitution, “are to be *decided by the people* in their sovereign capacity, or in regard to which *full discretionary authority* has been delegated to the legislative or executive branch

The 1987 Constitution, however, has narrowed the reach of the political question.  It has expanded the prerogatives of the judiciary by expressly vesting it with the power to determine grave abuse of discretion on the part of any branch or instrumentality of government.  This *express*  authority finds its roots in *Avelino v. Cuenco,*4 in which the Supreme Court — after some hesitation — decided that it had the duty and the corollary power to settle the question of who was the duly elected president of the Senate,  a matter normally within the exclusive realm of that chamber of the legislature.  Waxing both literary and philosophical, Justice Gregorio Perfecto, with courage admirable in those days of judicial restraint, boldly declared:

“Judicial ‘hands-off’ policy is, in effect, a showing of official inferiority complex.  Consequently, like its parallel in the psychological field, it is premised on notions of reality fundamentally wrong.  It is an upshot of distorted past experience, warping the mind so as to become unable to have a healthy appraisal of reality in its true form.”5

In exercising its collegial power to uphold the rule of law, the Supreme Court deals not only with the “why,” the “what” and “to what extent.”  It also deals with the “how” in formulating its decisions which, under our Constitution,7 shall state “clearly and distinctly the facts and the law on which [they are] based.”  In my lectures on “How to Write Decisions,” delivered during seminars sponsored by the Philippine Judicial Academy for lower court magistrates, I have always emphasized the need to observe the “4 Cs” of effective legal writing:  *correct, complete, clear* and *concise.*  The Supreme Court itself has expressed in the following words its exasperation over “extraordinary verbiage”:

“Kilometric decisions without much substance must be avoided, to be sure, but the other extreme, where substance is also lost in the wish to be brief, is no less unacceptable…  The ideal decision is that which, with welcome economy of words, arrives at the factual findings, reaches the legal conclusion,

By its nature, decision-writing is aimed mainly at settling a controversy at hand.  It is usually done by simply reciting the facts and then applying the relevant law.  But let it not be forgotten that many decisions are stories of our people’s life and faith, written not just for information; but also for refreshment, upliftment of spirit, and wisdom in many fields of human endeavor.  Sometimes, cases involve life-and-death issues, the interest of the entire country, or the crossroads of pulsating national events.

Thus, they cannot simply be disposed of in a cut-and-dried fashion.  Here, the intellectual and literary talents of the justices are summoned, and their writing skills put to a test.  The Supreme Court itself9 extols “pride of authorship” and exhorts its members to use a “little style” to “help liven the opinion trapped in the tortuous lexicon of the law with all its whereases and wherefores.  A judicial decision does not have to be a bore.”

Indeed, this three-volume publication of the Supreme Court’s *obra maestra* during the past 100 years demonstrates that far from being “bores,” not only are court judgments written in inspired and readable prose; they are also veritable sources of history, philosophy and literature.

Truly, the excerpts contained in this trilogy capture the transformation of the Supreme Court from a passive, laid back, generally cautious and conservative

While each of these three books is classified as literature or philosophy or history, there are many decisions that encapsulate all three essences — and more — in their hallowed pages.  The recent Decision upholding the legitimacy of Mrs. Gloria Macapagal Arroyo’s ascension to the Presidency and the Resolution denying the Motion for Reconsideration, as well as the various Separate Opinions therein, are leading examples of all three.10  The Court’s consciousness of the past, the present and the future is immortalized in *Morales v. Enrile*11 in these words:

“When the judgment of history is written, as leaders of our people, we shall be asked to account not only for what we did, not only for what we did not do, but also for what visions we have today of our tomorrow.”

In celebrating its 100 years of service to the nation, the Supreme Court has lined up more than 50 projects.  But in presenting to its various publics these three volumes which profile its finest “products,”  it cannot be prouder.  These books aim to prove that Supreme Court decisions qualify, not only as the final words in the settlement of disputes but also as gems of literature, philosophy and history.
