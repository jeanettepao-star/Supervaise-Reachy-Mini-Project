# Appendix A: A Salute to My Mentor

**I** would like to begin by calling attention to and elucidating my own knowledge of an incident mentioned on page 75 of the book being launched today, *A Journey of Struggle and Hope.*

I was a freshman at the Far Eastern University Institute of Law in 1956, the school year barely a few weeks old, when suddenly the law students, who were a thousand strong, declared a “strike” in protest of what they perceived was the precipitate and unjustified retirement of the well-loved and venerable dean of the law school.  As a new law student, I did not personally know our old dean, but I went along with the strikers as they filled the FEU auditorium with chants, slogans and anguished cries of “Persecution!”

Then, a youngish 36-year-old bespectacled professor, a Yale and Harvard graduate and bar topnotcher, walked to center stage, approached the microphone, and singlehandedly faced the hostile

crowd.  The new dean, Dr. Jovito R. Salonga, spoke with a forceful tremolo and held the rowdy and rambunctious crowd spellbound.  In thirty minutes flat, the huge crowd of activist students trooped back to their classrooms, convinced of the error in their mass action; and of the wisdom, the honesty, the sincerity and the good faith of their new dean.  Thus did I, along with many others, find the guru who would become my role model not only in my chosen career but, more important, in my life as a whole.

As a student, I was very active in extracurricular activities, having become president not only of the university student council, but also of the National Union of Students of the Philippines.  Because of these activities, I tended to give secondary importance to my academic studies.  But Dean Salonga constantly reminded me that my first duty was to excel in academics.

As my professor in evidence and corporation law, he would call me to recite when student activities were at their height — perhaps to test me whether I was adhering to my promise never to forget my academic preparation in the midst of the adulation of my leadership activities not only in FEU, but even in the wider arena of national student politics.  Because of his constant monitoring of my grades, I managed to graduate with Latin honors and as “most outstanding student.”

A few days prior to the 1960 bar examinations, I got sick.  To aggravate the situation, a rainstorm hit the city, and I had to wade across knee-deep waters on R. Papa Street from the FEU Hospital where I was confined, to the University of the East where the exams were being held.  After what I thought was a lackluster

first-day performance, I wanted to quit the examinations for the remaining days.  But Dean Salonga would not hear of it.  With his constant encouragement, I still managed to pass, copping the 6th highest place.

He invited me to join his prestigious law firm, which included as partners some of the best legal minds in the country like Sedfrey A. Ordoñez, Pedro L. Yap and Neptali A. Gonzales.  He taught me the rudiments of law practice; more significantly, he demonstrated the life-defining values of integrity, prudence and fairness.  My admiration and awe of Dr. Salonga grew even more.

Then and up to now, he is a living model of his teachings.  He taught by example, not by words.  He is a dedicated husband, a caring father, a faithful friend and a devoted man of God.  Never compromising his principles and values in exchange for friendship, kinship, relationship, wealth or power, he lives an almost ascetic life.  He does not smoke, does not drink, and does not gamble.  Money, worldly pleasures, titles and honors hold no fascination for him.

That we are of different religious faiths — he being a devout Protestant, a respected leader of the Cosmopolitan Church; and I, a fledgling Catholic — has not diminished at all our more than four decades of enriching friendship; or my own admiration, esteem and affection for him.  This is probably because we never discuss what separates us, but only what truly binds us.

Prior to my appointment to the highest court of the land, Senator Salonga accorded me — unconsciously, I am sure — the greatest honor in my

career as a practising lawyer, not by awarding me a plaque of gold or conferring on me an honorary degree.  Rather, he did so by asking me to be his personal legal counsel during his incumbency as Senate President and to be concurrently chief legal counsel of the Liberal Party which he then headed.  Nothing is more fulfilling, more exhilarating, than the honor of being counsel to one’s mentor and former boss!

As some of you may know, my ascension to the Supreme Court was not easy.  From 1992 to 1995, there were seven vacancies in the Court due to the compulsory retirement of incumbents.  For each of those seven vacancies, my name was submitted to the Judicial and Bar Council (JBC) for screening.  I failed the JBC test because of the alleged insufficiency of my professional competence and my alleged transgressions and sins.  But in all those trying three years, Senator Salonga was beside me, behind me, and even above me, cheering me up, tirelessly uplifting my sagging spirit and praying for me.  He would call me up frequently, assuring me of his support and unfailing faith and urging me never to lose trust in the faithfulness of God.  And whenever I felt really low and hopeless, he would patiently remind me of Romans 8:28.

*God makes all things work together*

*For the good of those who love Him*

*And are called according to His purpose.*

When finally I was nominated by the JBC and eventually appointed by President Ramos, he was the first to congratulate me not only for the appointment, but also for my keeping faith in the grace and bounty of the Lord!

In my conduct and work in the Supreme Court, I am proud to say that I have tried my best to follow and express my mentor’s ideas and ideals.  Those who read my decisions in the Court, as well as my writings and speeches elsewhere, will surely detect the legal philosophy and life values of Senator Salonga imbedded in them.  I try to echo, even faintly, his teaching that law cannot be separated from life, that it should be used as a brick in building the social edifice and as a means of fulfilling the deepest aspirations of man.

But his most significant teaching is not really about the law, as it is about life.  And if my presence today must have any relevance at all, I now would like to summarize the one teaching of my guru which I will always treasure, and it is this: While it is desirable to have some of the necessities that money *can* buy, like adequate food on the table, sufficient clothing and shelter for the family, sports and recreation for the body, and even a car and a house, I should never never forget that it is far more important to aspire for the things that money *cannot* buy, like excellence, integrity, honor, dignity and a reverential regard and love for God as the Source of all that is true, good and beautiful.

*Maraming salamat po.*
