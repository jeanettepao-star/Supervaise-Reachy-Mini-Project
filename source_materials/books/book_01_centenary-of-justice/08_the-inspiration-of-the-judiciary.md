# Chapter 8: The Inspiration of the Judiciary

*[During the regional centennial program in Region 7, Justice Panganiban was invited to deliver an “Inspirational Message” to motivate the judges and the employees in the region in the performance of their work. In response, he planed in to deliver this message entitled “Inspiration of the Judiciary” on January 27, 2001, at the Waterfront Hotel in Cebu City.]*

	THE INSPIRATION OF THE JUDICIARY	101

**A**t the outset, permit me to thank Judges Galicano C. Arriesgado, Isaias Dicdican and Pampio Abarintos for inviting me to attend this “Culmination Night” of the Seventh Judicial Region’s celebration of the Supreme Court Centenary. In responding to the Supreme Court’s call to celebrate its 100th year of service to the country, Region VII blazes the trail. Indeed, it is only fitting that your region should be the first. After all, Chief Justice Davide proudly hails from your area! Your success will reverberate all over the country, as the various regions undertake their own commemorations.

Judges Arriesgado et al. asked me to deliver “an inspirational message.” According to Webster’s International Dictionary, an inspiration is “a divine influence or action upon the lives of certain persons that is believed to qualify them to receive and communicate sacred revelation and is interpreted within Christianity as the direct action of the Holy Spirit.” In other words, through the power of inspiration, a person is able to receive and communicate sacred knowledge from God. This Christian inspiration is best exemplified by the four gospel writers in the Bible — Saints Matthew, Mark, Luke and John — who were inspired by God to write the Holy Scriptures which we, Christians, accept as the word of the Lord. I am sure that in inviting me, Judge Arriesgado did not believe that I was qualified to speak the word of God. Instead, he must have had in mind Webster’s other definition of inspiration, which is “an act or power of moving the intellect or emotion.”

In this light, inspiration is addressed to the mind or the heart or both. Indeed, ordinary mortals have

accomplished heroic and saintly deeds even beyond their wildest expectations, because they have been moved by the words and the lives of others. As Longfellow exhorts, “Lives of great men all remind us we can make our life sublime.” When we are inspired by some great purpose, by some extraordinary example, our consciousness expands in every direction and discovers greatness in ourselves, which we never dreamed we could transcend. To be inspired, therefore, is to be led by the example of another person, a model human being whom we admire, esteem and love. Indeed, true leadership is sourced from example and inspiration, not from force and domination.

In the judiciary to which we all belong, we need not look far and long, because that great model or inspiration is our own Chief Justice Hilario G. Davide Jr., who inspires and leads us by his own example in at least four ways: his integrity, simplicity, dedication to duty and unbending faith in God. Let me explain them one by one.

*First*, he is a towering persona of integrity. In his ten years as a jurist and in many more before as a legislator, constitution-maker and elections chairman, no one has ever doubted his honesty. No taint of corruption has ever been hurled at him or his family. Kinship, relationship or friendship has never interfered with his work. Indeed, he has no business cronies, no favored friends, no officious relatives, not even backdoor coteries to prop him up in times of difficulty and crisis. To have integrity implies moral courage, which means not only theoretical bravery in battle but, a priori, a purity of spirit and a clarity of conscience. For only he who is pure of heart and clear of mind

with no skeletons to hide can rightfully be called a man of integrity like our Chief.

What political will is to a sovereign head, moral courage is to a magistrate. A judge may have memorized all the statutes and regulations; he may know by heart the Constitution and the codes; he may have imprinted on his brain the UN Charter and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. But if he has no moral courage — if he does not have the willingness to battle the establishment; to carry on in spite of seemingly insurmountable opposition; to be a beacon for the weak, the oppressed and the disadvantaged; and to stand for his conviction against all odds, even against all his colleagues and the entire government machinery put together — he is really nothing.

And, I assure you ladies and gentlemen, moral courage born of integrity our Chief has an abundance of — more than enough to inspire each one of us, 1,000 strong, in this ballroom tonight!

*Second*, our millennial Chief Justice lives simply, frugally and transparently. He does not drink, does not smoke, does not gamble, and rarely attends social occasions outside official functions. He sleeps early and also wakes up early — about 3:30 a.m. Upon waking up, he prays. Then he cuts the Bible and reads at random the page that opens. It is his firm belief that he is simply the Lord’s servant who gets his day-to-day instruction from God’s book. Thereafter he indulges in his favorite sport and exercise, which is brisk walking. He eats a fast breakfast and, before the break of dawn, he is on his way to office.

It is easy to get along with him because he is humble and soft-spoken, almost self-deprecating, but

at the same time self-actualized. During our more than five years together in the Court, I have yet to hear him raise his voice or blurt out nasty words. He is aware of who he is and of what he can or cannot do. He has a vision of what needs to be done and a clear concept of how and when to accomplish it.

*Third*, Chief Justice Davide is dedicated to whatever he is doing. Among the first to arrive, he reports to office at 5:30 a.m. Until evening he works ceaselessly. Besides being an avid and a fast reader, he has an amazing capacity to absorb and internalize facts as well as to analyze legal provisions. He is always punctual in his appointments and always delivers what he commits. When the impeachment trial was still going on, many people were amazed how he could accomplish his work as Chief Justice in the morning and, in the afternoon until evening, preside over the Impeachment Court — both in a brilliant and faultless manner.

As a jurist, he is always prepared during deliberations of the Court — whether to defend his *ponencias* or to challenge those of others. Quite easily he can digest the minutiae of judicial work, for which he has an elephantine memory and a gluttonous appetite. He writes exhaustively, patiently explaining the details of the cases assigned to him, stating all the arguments of each of the parties, and then discussing one by one each question raised. Dissecting issues with a surgeon’s scalpel, he rules clearly and firmly, leaving no doubt or equivocation or residue. Seldom does he pontificate; instead, he argues his points with a view to convince, sometimes in a manner that overwhelms the reader. He avoids obiters, flamboyance and braggadocio; rather, he writes

unaffectedly, without much flourish or pretension. Nevertheless, his balance and symmetry are identifiable and poised.

And *fourth*, he is a very spiritual and humanistic person, a firm believer in our Lord Jesus Christ. Looking back at his own appointment more than two years ago, Chief Justice Davide admits that he was himself pleasantly surprised at the unconventional way he was appointed and sworn into office. While profusely thankful to the President, at the same time he felt that his elevation bore the indelible imprint of the Almighty.

That was November 30, 1998. On that day he had awakened at 3:30 a.m., as before. After his usual morning prayer, he randomly cut the New American Bible which opened to the Second Book of Maccabees, Chapter 10. The title of the chapter, “Purification of the Temple and the City,” immediately struck him. Inspired that this was God’s message, he made it his mission to purify and cleanse the judiciary of the misfit, the corrupt and the unqualified. At the same time, he promised to welcome and reward the competent and the ethical. Truly, he has been relentless in his effort to establish in this country a new temple of justice befitting God and man.

Exactly a week ago, on Saturday, January 20, 2001, he woke up early as usual. Again, he prayed and cut the Bible. It opened to the Book of Isaiah, Chapter 62, which discussed the “Restoration of Zion.” Reflecting on the then ongoing EDSA People Power, it dawned upon him that that particular day would witness the restoration of God’s kingdom in the Philippines. And so, on that very day, he thought of inducting Her Excellency, Gloria Macapagal Arroyo, as the 14th

As I end this short talk, let me exhort all of you, my fellow workers in the judiciary, to follow the example of our Chief Justice. In fact, the role model, hope and inspiration of almost every Filipino today is our centennial Chief who, according to the most authoritative poll surveys, is the most admired citizen today. No wonder, the normally critical and finicky *Philippine Daily Inquirer* has acclaimed him as the Filipino of the Year! Let his unsullied integrity and compelling moral courage, his simple and transparent lifestyle, his unfailing devotion to duty, and his unbending faith in our Lord Jesus Christ be our constant inspiration in performing our tasks and enjoying a life worth living, not only for the here and now, but also for the hereafter and forever.

Thank you.
