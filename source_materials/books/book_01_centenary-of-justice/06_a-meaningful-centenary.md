# Chapter 6: A Meaningful Centenary

*[The Supreme Court centenary is commemorated not only in Manila, but also in all judicial regions and by all courts and court personnel all over the Philippines. Under the direct supervision of the court administrator and the executive judges in the regions, the trial courts and their employees gathered by the thousands and celebrated by means of motorcades, cultural programs, convocations and rallies. In most of the regional celebrations, Justice Panganiban, as overall chair, flew in to deliver messages. He exhorted his audience to celebrate not just with “temporary merriment,” but also with a pledge to implement the judicial reforms the Supreme Court was espousing. Typical of these speeches was the following address he delivered in the Baguio City Convention Center on April 4, 2001, during the joint celebration of Judicial Regions 1 and 2.]*

88    A CENTENARY OF JUSTICE

**O**n behalf of the Supreme Court and the Centenary Executive Committee, I welcome all of you today. I am pleased to see the many radiant faces — at least three thousand — in this Hall, a sign, I take it, that all the officials and employees in Regions 1 and 2 are happy and contented.

I realize that most of you who live and work outside Baguio have come here today at great effort and expense. I appreciate your attendance and cooperation in making our regional centenary celebrations a rousing success.

At the outset, let me just clarify that the judiciary reckons its founding on June 11, 1901, because it was on this date that Act No. 136, otherwise known as the Judiciary Law, was enacted by the Second Philippine Commission. This law “vested judicial power xxx in the Supreme Court, Courts of First Instance and Justice of the Peace Courts and such other courts as may be authorized by law.”

A year earlier, on May 29, 1899, the then American military governor, Gen. Elwell Otis, issued General Order No. 20 which created a “Supreme Court” and designated its members. However, the said Order was “really nothing more than an extension of the military authority of the United States Army.”

On the other hand, Act No. 136 was approved by a civil government; equally important, it established an independent judicial hierarchy, with the Supreme Court as the apex. Since we treasure independence as a basic feature of our judicial system, we have chosen June 11, 1901, as our foundation date. Thus, on June 11, 2001, the Supreme Court and the judiciary will be 100 years old.

To properly celebrate this once-in-a lifetime occasion and to fittingly remember the many great and heroic men and women who have graced the bench, the Supreme Court formed the Centenary Executive Committee, which I have the honor of chairing.

The Committee has lined up more than 50 projects and programs. They involve all types of activities — intellectual, cultural, historical, educational and physical. Participants include different sectors of society, especially judges and other judicial personnel, lawyers, educators, students, media persons, other government officials and employees, and the public at large in all regions of the country, from the Ilocos area to the islands of Sulu and Tawi-tawi.

Our goals are, *first,* to communicate to everyone the role of the judiciary in nation-building during the last 100 years; and *second,* to focus on and encourage participation in the ongoing judicial reform program of the Supreme Court. Our overall theme is “Katarungan at Bayan, Magpakailanman.” By this, we stress our allegiance to only one cause, justice; and to only one entity, the nation.

While our centenary celebrations involve all types of activities and include not only the judiciary, but also the general public throughout the country, the Executive Committee is keenly proud of our regional centenary celebrations like that which we are having today. After all, the judiciary is composed of not only the Supreme Court and the other appellate courts, but also the trial courts all over the Philippines. In fact, trial courts really constitute the backbone of the judicial branch of government. If I may compare the judiciary with the military, I would say that trial judges, clerks of court, sheriffs, process servers and other judicial

personnel are equivalent to the marines and the foot soldiers in the front line of the battlefield. Indeed, it is you who encounter, face to face, the weary litigants, their sometimes arrogant lawyers, the critical media and the expectant general public. Indeed, to the masses of our country, you are the sparkling representatives of the judiciary and the veritable symbols of justice itself.

In this context, I am very happy to note the extensive and all-out participation of all front liners in Regions I and II, as shown by the massive audience of 3,000 warm bodies we have today. Truly, it is exhilarating to get together and celebrate our centenary with sports contests, parades, merriment, fellowship, as well as cultural and educational programs. On this occasion, however, we should also remind ourselves of and participate in the judicial reforms the Supreme Court is pushing forward. Of the many changes the High Court is sponsoring, perhaps none is more visible and important to the public than the speedy dispensation of quality justice.

Delay in the disposition of court cases has been a longstanding complaint not only in the Philippines, but also in many other countries around the world. The International Herald Tribune, in its June 2, 2000 issue (p. 12), reports that in one Asian country, “[a]bout 25 million cases are pending, a breathtaking pile up of untried accused and unsatisfied aggrieved. By one calculation, even if no new actions were filed, 324 years would be needed to clear the dockets.”

Fortunately, the problem of clogged dockets in our country is not as bad as that reported in the *Herald Tribune.* For one thing, the Supreme Court has worked hard to reduce its own backlog. By *backlog* I refer to

cases that were deemed submitted for the decision of the Court more than twenty-four months after the last pleading had been filed. Twenty-four months is the period mandated by the Constitution for the highest Tribunal; for the trial courts, three months. Five years ago, when I joined the Court, we had about 2,000 overdue cases. As of today, April 4, 2001, we have been able to reduce this to about 300 only. We hope to resolve most, if not all, of these and thus completely eliminate our backlog or reduce it to an insignificant number by June 11, 2001, our 100th anniversary.

Without meaning to splash cold water on our warm celebrations today, let me add, however, as a measure of transparency, that the picture in the lower courts is not as good. In my latest book, *Transparency, Unanimity **&** Diversity* (2000 ed.), I reported that in the Court of Appeals (CA), for the five-year period 1995-1999, with a yearly average of 8,907 new cases filed and 8,290 decided, there were more than 3,000 (3,088) cases newly filed (8,907) than those decided.

In the second-level courts, which include the Regional Trial Courts (RTC), the Court of Tax Appeals (CTA) and the Shari’ah District Courts (SDC), the case inflow for the same five-year period (1995-1999) was nearly 850,000 (848,329), while the case outflow was only about 815,000 (814,354). The total increase, then, was almost 35,000 for the said period. Now, that’s a lot of piled-up cases!

The situation is no better in the first-level courts, which include Metropolitan Trial Courts (MeTC), Municipal Trial Courts in Cities (MTCC), Municipal Circuit Trial Courts (MCTC), Municipal Trial Courts (MTC) and Shari’ah Circuit Courts (SCC). During the same period (1995-1999), there was an increase of

almost 65,000 cases per year or a humongous total of almost 325,000 (324,174) for the last five years.

I mention these statistics not to condemn you, but merely to remind you, our esteemed partners in the judiciary, of one persistent public complaint, which is delay in case adjudication. I know that many problems, sometimes outside our control, caused this huge increase in pending cases. For one thing, one third of all trial courts are vacant, due largely to the fact that there are not enough qualified applicants. Indeed, the low salaries, coupled with the unfavorable peace and order conditions in outlying areas, have discouraged many capable lawyers from joining the judiciary.

For another, we need better judicial tools and facilities. To partially solve this problem, the Supreme Court has already authorized the purchase of computers for *all* regional trial courts and selected metropolitan and municipal courts all over the Philippines. These computers will be distributed soon, together with up-to-date software, to enable judges to access all decisions of the Supreme Court from 1901 to the present.

Then, of course, we know that because of *Batas Pambansa Bilang* 22, first level courts are swamped with bouncing checks cases. This is why the Supreme Court has issued a Circular giving trial courts some guidelines in imposing penalties for the violation of this law. We would like to discourage the use of the court system as a collection agency for rubber checks. Certainly, the banking system should find some other ways of solving the problem of unpaid checks. For instance, habitual bad check writers could be banned from opening and maintaining checking accounts.

This is the practice in the United States and many other developed countries.

Aside from our effort to eliminate delays in court cases, there are many other reforms being highlighted during our centenary celebrations. Many of these reforms aim to improve legal and ethical standards. Some intend to recruit and properly pay “national professors of law” who will be assigned to various law schools, which otherwise cannot afford to pay them socially relevant compensation. Still some other reforms aspire to provide continuing education for judges and other judicial personnel; to construct Halls of Justice in all judicial regions, consistent with the dignity and high office of judges; to raise the salaries of judges and other judicial personnel by exempting the judiciary from the clutches of the Salary Standardization Law; to improve the judicial disciplinary process; and to encourage the use of alternative dispute resolution methods. I do not have the time to explain all of these reform measures, but soon you will be hearing, seeing and, I hope, participating in many of them.

But let me take this occasion to appeal to all of you to help the Supreme Court in its effort to institute these much needed reforms. You can start by helping reduce, if not eliminate, your own roster of delayed cases. If the Supreme Court can eliminate its own backlog, I know the lower courts will be able to follow suit.

One way to do this is for judges to individually attend to their own backlogs, to hold hearings regularly and diligently or otherwise to get the parties to settle their controversies amicably, and to render judgments and orders within the reglementary period.

I know that if we have the will and the determination, we will all be victorious in the battle for swift, transparent and quality justice in our country.

As we celebrate our centenary, we have every reason to be proud of the excellent record of the judiciary during the last 100 years. It has been a dynamic, active and independent institution that has an enviable record of competence and integrity. It now enjoys unprecedented public attention. Because of the well-publicized Senate impeachment trial, our people have become very much aware of the importance of judicial proceedings. Legal phrases like “Your Honor,” “Immaterial and irrelevant,” “Objection sustained” and “Objection overruled” have become part of the lay person’s vocabulary.

Equally important, we have a Chief Justice whose integrity, ability and sterling performance as presiding officer of the impeachment trial has been universally acclaimed. Today, he is rated as the most credible Filipino; and the institution he represents — the Supreme Court — enjoys the highest approval rating in all public polling surveys.

Indeed, as members of the judiciary, we stand proud and honored during our centenary. But we cannot rest on these laurels. We must continue to work hard, improve our performance even more, and meet the high expectations of our countrymen.

As I close my little message today, let me recite one of my favorite prayers composed by the late Bishop Benny Tudtud, entitled “Disturb Us O Lord.” I believe it is relevant to all of us, who have persevered in our work and achieved some success in our careers, to remind us not to be complacent with our minuscule
