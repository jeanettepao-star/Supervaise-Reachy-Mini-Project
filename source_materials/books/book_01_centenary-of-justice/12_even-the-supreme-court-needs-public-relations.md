# Chapter 12: Even the Supreme Court Needs Public Relations

*[“This chapter is taken from the keynote address of Justice Panganiban during the Anvil Awards sponsored by the Public Relations Society of the Philippines (PRSP) on February 23, 2001, at the Hotel Intercontinental in Makati City.  Although delivered during an event not directly connected with the centenary, it is nonetheless included in this book, because it explains the present Supreme Court policy of transparency.  It also discusses Justice Panganiban’s role as chair of the centenary celebrations.]*

**O**n behalf of the board of judges, which I have the honor of chairing, I congratulate all the winners of the Anvil Awards sponsored by the Public Relations Society of the Philippines (PRSP).  To be sure, all the entries were unquestionably of very high quality.  But choices had to be made, and the judges carefully and meticulously chose the winners.

The judges are particularly proud of the Grand Anvil winner — the Intel Philippines Science Fair.  I believe that it vividly demonstrates corporate selflessness.  As a nationwide competition to discover and recognize our best student-scientists, it focuses on the country’s need to orient our young people to science and technology, which are essential to our individual and national prosperity in the new century.

This does not necessarily mean that the others which rated second best were less than outstanding.  Quite the contrary, all the finalists for the Grand Anvil were truly excellent models of responsible corporate citizenship.  Most of the entries did not directly promote the products or the services of the sponsors, but were aimed more at inculcating civic consciousness and developing the potentials of our people and of our country as a whole.

Our task as judges was not easy.  We had to individually read, view and evaluate over a hundred entries.  Then we met and deliberated collegially in all seriousness during three long evenings.  At one time, I remarked that with the dedication, the focus and the passion displayed, the deliberations of the board of judges were comparable to those of the Supreme Court en banc except that, during the Anvil sessions, there were more jokes and relaxing one-liners interjected than during the more somber sessions of the High Tribunal.

In any event, let me recognize the judges, whom I am requesting to stand as I call their names alphabetically:  Ms Corazon S. Alvina, Ms Luchi Arguelles, Ms Candace T. Giron, Mr. Philip R. Ives, Mr. James S. Ong, Mr. Gary Z. Pimentel, Mr. Winston A. Marbella, Atty. Melvyn S. Martin, Mr. Edgar S. San Luis, Ms Victoria Segovia and Amb. Bienvenido A. Tan Jr.

May I also congratulate the PRSP, especially its president — the hard-working and well-liked Gen. Honesto M. Isleta, APR; as well as  the Anvil Awards Committee headed by the indefatigable Evelyn M. del Rosario, APR.  *Palakpakan po natin sila.*

Aside from heading the Anvil board of judges, I was also requested by Ms Evelyn del Rosario to deliver a few remarks tonight as guest of honor and speaker.  I accepted this added responsibility for three reasons.

*First,* my wife, Leni, who is the real chief of this justice and who is present tonight, is the daughter of the late Jose A. Carpio Sr., the founding father and first president of the Public Relations Society of the Philippines.  He was also the first Filipino to be elected to active membership in the Public Relations Society of America.  As most of you probably know, he is credited with having elevated public relations to a profession on the same level as law, medicine, engineering and the priesthood.  He espoused corporate social consciousness as a vital function of public relations.  His crusade for responsible corporate citizenship culminated in the organization of Philippine Business for Social Progress, the acknowledged leader of corporate social responsibility in our country.  Best of all, his family will always be grateful for the grant to

him of the first1 “Gawad Panday,” the highest honor conferred by PRSP, in recognition of his being the “Dean of Filipino PR Practitioners.”

On a personal basis, I should add that Mr. Carpio nearly succeeded in convincing me during my fledgling years, while still an underpaid and overworked law practitioner, to join his public relations firm as executive vice president.  Looking back, I could have followed in his footsteps in this profession, had it not been for my *Kapampangan* stubborness that made me want to stand on my own two feet, instead of relying on the generosity of my kind father-in-law.

*Second,* my fascination with public relations did not end with the demise of Mr. Carpio in 1989.  Even now, the many PR concepts and principles I learned from him serve me in good stead as a member of the Supreme Court.  Two years ago it organized, for the first time in its 100-year history, a Committee on Public Information which I was asked to chair.

Until recently, the Supreme Court was probably the most inscrutable government institution in this country.  It was regarded as a fortress, a Mount Olympus where the gods dwelt far above and removed from the teeming crowd.

Our Code of Judicial Ethics limits the public appearances of justices, who are expected to socialize mainly among themselves only.  They are viewed as beings who are apart and removed from the mundane concerns of daily living.  And yet, they are called upon

1  Together with President Fidel V. Ramos, Mr. Carpio was posthumously given this Award on August 31, 1998, at the Social Hall of the Development Bank of the Philippines.

to decide questions and issues that involve the lives, the liberties, the properties and the relationships of the people at large.  Certainly, both the Court and its members could not forever resist the intrusion of the Information Age or the curiosity — not to say the prying and preying eyes and ears — of an awakened, technology-driven and people-powered citizenry.

I must add that the need for the judiciary to be better understood and appreciated is not merely a Philippine phenomenon.  It is a worldwide trend.  Most judicial authorities in Europe and America realize the need for public information to improve their relationship with their various publics.  Even the very conservative Supreme Courts of the United States and of Great Britain have found the need to properly inform the public of their actions and pronouncements.

While its specific decisions are nonpolitical and thus not subject to popular vote, much less to referendums, the Supreme Court nonetheless realizes that it can be effective only insofar as it retains the long-term support of the public.  Indeed, a litigation is won, not because one side gets more cheers from the gallery, but because it is upheld by the law through a process of reasoning.

At the same time, we know that public acceptance of the Court’s actions and decisions — especially those that go against public opinions and perceptions — is possible only if the Tribunal enjoys the people’s residual respect and esteem, which are engendered when people know enough of its members and their work.  To be appreciated and defended, the Supreme Court realizes that it must first be understood by our people.  And this is possible only if it adheres to a policy of

transparency by systematically enabling the people to know its actions, programs and work.  In short, even the Supreme Court needs public relations.

*Third,* I asked the Court to create a Public Information Office and summoned professional full-time help, first by inviting Atty. Ismael G. Khan Jr. to head it.  Then there was the effort to orient the justices themselves on the need for transparency, for issuing decisions in simpler language with less legalese, and for more openness to public scrutiny without losing the mystique and the respect due our offices.

I would like to report very briefly on what the Supreme Court’s Committee on Public Information has done during the last two years.  As soon as I was appointed Public Information chair, I defined our objective as simply “to tell others the truth about the Supreme Court and its work.”  It is not to “sanitize” or to “angle” stories.  Neither is it to “proselytize” and “propaganize,” much less to spin tales or embroider the Court’s work.  Our goal is simple: to tell our various publics the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth.

Further, I thought the Court was an abstract institution that needed a human face.  So I looked upon the Chief Justice as the representation or human symbol of the institution.  To make that symbol known, I wrote a book about him shortly after his assumption of office in 1998.  I entitled it *Leadership by Example: The Davide Standard,* 3,000 copies of which were distributed to decision-makers, government officials, media personalities, libraries, business leaders, non-government organizations and civic groups.  In November 2000, I followed this up with another

volume, *Transparency, Unanimity **&** Diversity,* which dwelt on the judicial reform program of the Supreme Court.  We also opened a web page that contained up-to-date information about the Court, its members and their work.  In early December 2000, during many radio-TV interviews in which there were questions on Hilario G. Davide Jr., I simply pointed to these publications and web site, adding, “I assure you that Chief Justice Davide will preside over the impeachment trial in a manner all of us Filipinos will be proud of.”

It is not really difficult to speak or write about our Chief Justice, because he personifies what I call the four “Ins” of an ideal magistrate; namely, integrity, intelligence, independence and industry.  In short, he is the model judge.  His irreproachable conduct during proceedings of the recent Senate Impeachment Court made him, according to poll surveys of SWS and Pulse Asia, the most trusted Filipino today.  Indeed, the cautious and choosy *Philippine Daily Inquirer* has named him “Filipino of the Year 2000.”  In the process, the Supreme Court, which he heads and represents, has grown in public esteem as the most credible public institution in the country today.

But having a great leader is not enough.  The institution itself must meet public expectations for speedy quality justice.  Hence, we have availed ourselves of various public relations programs and tools to push the judicial reforms the Supreme Court is sponsoring.  We view the 100th anniversary of its founding on June 11, 2001 as a fitting occasion to focus on the role of the judiciary in nation-building and to push our reform agenda.  This reform program has caught the attention and the support not only of our countrymen, but also of multinational and foreign

developmental agencies like the World Bank, UNDP, USAID, Canadian CIDA, the Dutch and the Belgian governments, Australian AID, Asian Development Bank, Asia Foundation and many others.

The Supreme Court’s Centenary Executive Committee, which I also have the honor of chairing, has lined up more than 50 projects and activities to commemorate our centennial.  I do not have the time to describe each one of these.  Let me just say that they include all types of activities — intellectual, cultural, historical, educational, physical and structural — to be participated in actively by all our publics, including judges and other judicial personnel, lawyers, students, media persons, and the general populace in all regions of the country.  These activities will be held over a period of one year.  Shortly, you should be hearing, seeing, reading and participating in some of them.  So, *abangan!*

Ladies and gentlemen, especially the public relations practitioners present today, let me conclude this address with a call to cooperation.  By this time, you probably realize that public relations and judicial dispensation have a basic commonality — the search for truth.  The ultimate goal of the Supreme Court, the entire judiciary and the law profession is to discover the truth and make it prevail.  I know that you share this same objective, because the first duty of the public relations profession is to bring the truth to light, and then to make it known to all.  There are various techniques, methods and tools to make the public know, appreciate and act on this truth.  And this is where the Supreme Court needs your expertise — to assist us in disseminating the truth, especially during our centenary.  I therefore seek your cooperation in disseminating the work of the Court through the tested programs and tools of your profession.

Let me end by congratulating you again.  *Mabuhay ang* PRSP *at ang* Anvil Awards.

*Salamat po.*
