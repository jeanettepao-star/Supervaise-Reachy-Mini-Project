# Chapter 1: A Renaissance in the Judiciary

*	[The sixth year of Justice Panganiban’s incumbency in the Supreme Court — October 11, 2000 to October 10, 2001 — was highlighted by the Court’s observance of its centenary.  Although the exact 100**th** birthday of the Court was on June 11, 2001, the celebrations were started one year before that day.  And recently, Chief Justice Hilario G. Davide Jr. has even extended the celebrations one year more — until June 11, 2002.  Lined up to mark the centenary are more than 50 activities and projects, which are being superintended and monitored by the Centennial Executive Committee chaired by Justice Panganiban.  Of these, none has attracted more attention and appreciation from the judiciary’s intellectual public than the Court’s Centenary Lecture Series.  It was hailed by Justice Panganiban as the fulcrum of the celebrations when, on June 11, 2001, right after the last lecture of Phase 1 of the Series, he delivered the Closing-Statement, which is reproduced as Chapter 1 of this book.]*

4          A CENTENARY OF JUSTICE

**T**he Centenary Lecture Series is one of more than 50 activities and projects that the Supreme Court is sponsoring to celebrate its 100th anniversary.  And today’s event marks the final lecture of Phase I in this monthly intellectual pursuit which began on July 11 last year, when former Chief Justice Andres R. Narvasa delivered the first one entitled “The Chief Justices in Philippine History.”  The lectures that followed discussed the achievements of the Philippine judiciary during the last one hundred years.  Thus, retired Chief Justice Enrique M. Fernando spoke of “A Century of Constitutionalism: The Mission (per Malcom, J.) and Its Fulfillment (per Laurel, J.).”

	During the past one hundred years also, the women on the bench were not to be deprived.  They, too, contributed their well-deserved share of the judiciary’s glory.  Though outnumbered, the lady justices were never out-written, out-argued or out-achieved by the men.  This outstanding fact was spelled out by Madame Justice Ameurfina A. Melencio Herrera in her lecture entitled “Feminine Grace, the High Court and Jurisprudence.”

	Our Muslim brothers and sisters were not forgotten.  In his lecture on September 12, 2000, Professor Michael O. Mastura, a former member of the House of Representatives, explained the relations between the “Shari’ah Law and the Philippine Legal System.”

	Law is territorial in its scope.  Nonetheless, its basic principles — especially those on human rights and civil liberties — are universal.  On this verity, we listened most auspiciously to two foreign legal luminaries: first, Justice Dorit Beinisch of the Israeli Supreme Court who, even in the midst of disruption and violence in her country, spoke fervently of “Protecting Civil Liberties in a State of Continuing Emergency”; and second, Dr. Pal Solt, President of the Hungarian Supreme Court who eloquently explained the “Function of the Supreme Court of Justice in Hungary,” a state that had emerged triumphantly from political and economic crises not too long ago.

	The current trends in jurisprudence were dissected in four well-attended lectures entitled (1) “Contracts and Transactions in E-Commerce” by Congressman Leandro B. Verceles Jr.; (2) “Life Technologies and the Rule of Law” by Dr. Franklin M. Zweig of the Albert Einstein Institute for Science, Health and the Courts; (3) “Old Doctrines and New Paradigms” by an incumbent member of the Court whose name need not be mentioned; and (4) “The Impact of People Power on Our Legal System” by a panel composed of Dean Raul Pangalangan, Atty. Katrina Legarda and Professor Randy David.

	As I said earlier, today marks the culminating lecture in our pre-anniversary series.  It is significant that, while the lecture round was started by the immediate past Chief Justice, it is now closed by the incumbent Chief; and while the first lecturer spoke of the past, and those in between extolled the present, the last one dwelt largely on the future — on his vision of “a judiciary that is independent, effective and efficient, and worthy of public trust and confidence; and on a legal profession that provides quality, ethical, accessible and cost-effective legal service to our people.”

	Indeed, the “Davide Watch” — a brushwork of the judiciary at the threshold of the new millennium,

announced by the Chief Justice immediately upon his assumption of office on November 30, 1998— was inspired by Scripture, born of idealism, but grounded on the reality of the possible.

	The Davide program for the judiciary and the legal profession is not meant to be merely a broad statement of intent and principles.  It is complemented by a definite development plan implemented day by day, pursuant to an ongoing Action Program for Judicial Reform (APJR).  Notably, the Program is actively supported not only by the Philippine government and local donor groups, but also by international development bodies like the United Nations Development Fund, the World Bank, the Asia Foundation, UNICEF, the Asian Development Bank and government-to-government assistance from several countries including Japan, Canada, Australia, the United States and the Netherlands.

	The entire centenary celebrations are focused not only on the contributions of the judiciary in nation-building during the last one hundred years but, more significantly, on the reforms that Chief Justice Davide has spoken about so enthusiastically in his lecture.  Indeed, a celebration as momentous as a centenary cannot be justified by a mere rehash of past successes or by an overextended gloating over the present.  Verily, it must set forth our collective dreams of the future — a future that glories in the well-earned achievements of the past and builds on the bright hopes for posterity.

	By his long-term vision, commitment to excellence and ethics, untiring dedication to duty, unquestioned integrity, as well as simple and transparent life and work style, Chief Justice Davide has unleashed the long-dormant will of everyone in the judiciary.  I am confident that under his leadership, we will all transcend our puny limitations and achieve far greater success than we all are capable of imagining.

	I cannot close our program today without thanking Justice Ameurfina A. Melencio Herrera and her co-workers in the Philippine Judicial Academy — specially retired Justice Antonio M. Martinez, Dr. Purificacion V. Quisumbing, and the others who are responsible for this highly successful lecture series.  Although we have more than 49 other centennial activities, none has probably whipped up more attention and appreciation from the Court’s intellectual public than the Centenary Lecture Series.

	For today’s lecture, I also thank our co-sponsors; namely, the Court of Appeals, University of Sto. Tomas, International Law Association and Lawasia.

	Ladies and gentlemen, “the trouble with hello is goodbye” – so goes the familiar strains of a song popularized many years ago.  It philosophizes about all things on earth having to come to an end.  It rhapsodizes on an unsaid tragedy woven by time and space on this finite earth.  It teaches that every novel must close, every journey must end, and every theater must draw its curtains.

	I say, however, that while our pre-centenary lecture series must conclude today, its influence, its reach and its memory will not end.  In this sense, this final lecture will live on as a testament to the long-term vision of our great Chief and the common dream of this august Court.  Indeed, today, no curtain will be drawn; our journey will not screech to an end.  Our judicial reform program, which speaks boldly of hope,

shall not just be for the nonce — engraved as it is in the granite edifices we are building and in the hearts and minds of a grateful people, fulfilled in the rule of law and strengthened by the agelessness of ongoing inspiration.

Indeed, the end product of our encounter today and during the whole lecture series will be a well-remembered renaissance in the judiciary, brought about by the vision of our leader and by the labor of his inspired colleagues.

I thank you.
