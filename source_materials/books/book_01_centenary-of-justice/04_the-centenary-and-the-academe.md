# Chapter 4: The Centenary and the Academe

*[**A majority of the more than 50 centennial projects and activities are dedicated to the improvement of the judiciary.  However, there are at least four programs that are intended to address the academe — law schools and law students.  These were explained by Justice Panganiban in a speech he delivered during the Centennial Debate between Ateneo de Manila and Silliman University on February 9, 2001, at Dumaguete City.*

*Although that debate was noncompetitive (no winner was adjudged), the competitive ****National Speech Festival**** referred to by the good justice in this speech actually took place.  During the finals of the ****National Oratorical Contest,**** held on June 7, 2001 in the Supreme Court Session Hall, the following won: Claude Albert Morales, University of the Philippines, overall champion; Andre A. Fornier, Ateneo de Manila University, first runner-up; and Cynette Lovelyn Mirasol, University of San Agustin, second runner-up.  In the ****National Debate,**** Xavier University in Cagayan de Oro (represented by Marlowe Selecios, Jo-Shancy Bermudez and Nueva Tagarda) emerged as the national champion; while the University of the Philippines (represented by Arnell Uychoco, Michael Mejia and Diane Desierto) was runner-up.  The ****Essay Contest**** produced the following winners: Margarita Eugenio F. Victorino, (FEU), first place; Jan Kendrick Z. Velasco (FEU), second place; and Adrian R. Montemayor (UST), third place.*

*Justice Panganiban’s speech at Dumaguete, reproduced hereunder, explains these centenary projects for the academe.]*

**A**ll the eight debaters tonight — four Ateneans and four Sillimanians — are eloquent, persuasive and forceful.  Do you agree?  And the audience, too, is excellent and responsive!  This is the first time I find people — 800 strong here in Luce Auditorium — who paid an admission fee (P50 each) to be able to witness a debate.  I thought such an aberration would happen only during PBA basketball games or during concerts of Regine Velasquez!

For a change, I am glad that I did not have to act as judge, but only as guest speaker.  Otherwise, I would have had to make a choice and thus disappoint some of the debaters.  Happily, I leave that difficult chore to the very capable Board of Judges composed of Sen. Franklin Drilon, the erstwhile Senate President who chose dignity and integrity over position and possessions; Rep. Jacinto Paras; and Dr. Henry Sojor, president of the Central Visayas Polytechnic College.

I will just await these excellent debaters when, after they pass — nay, top — the bar examinations, they will appear as lawyers in the Supreme Court.  Until then, I reserve the opportunity to judge them.

In the meantime, on behalf of the Supreme Court, let me thank Silliman University and Ateneo de Manila for helping celebrate the SC centenary via this grand debate.  My special appreciation goes to Executive Judge Eleuterio E. Chiu and Dean Myles Nicholas G. Behar of the Silliman College of Law for inviting me to witness this outstanding demonstration of forensic skills.

To commemorate its 100th anniversary which falls on June 11, 2001, the Supreme Court has lined up over fifty programs and activities for a period of one year

starting June 11 last year.  The centenary celebrations include all types of activities — intellectual, cultural, historical, educational, physical and structural — participated in by all sectors of society including educators, students, media persons, judges, lawyers, court employees and the general public in all regions of the Philippines.

Our goals are, *first,* to communicate to the whole country the role of the judiciary in nation-building; and *second,* to focus on and encourage participation in the ongoing judicial reform program of the Supreme Court.  Our overall theme is “*Katarungan at Bayan, Magpakailanman.*”  By this, we emphasize our allegiance to one cause,  justice; and to one entity, the nation.

Due to limitations of time, I will not be able to discuss all the more than fifty centenary activities I referred to earlier.  But I would like to call your attention to four programs that have immediate impact on law schools and law students.

*First,* the Supreme Court, through its Centenary Executive Committee, inspired the formation of the Foundation for the Enhancement of Legal Education, chaired by Atty. Ricardo J. Romulo.  The Court has committed itself to contributing the seed fund of P10 million; for its part, the Foundation will raise P40 million, for a total initial fund of P50 million, which will be used as an endowment for the establishment of national professorial chairs in law, as well as fellowships and scholarships for deserving but poor law students.

With the help of this Foundation, the Court hopes to elevate the standards of legal education by providing adequately paid, competent, full-time

professors to law schools that cannot afford to hire them on their own meager resources.  Schools like Ateneo and Silliman may not have as much need for these national professors, because such schools can more than adequately afford their own full-time mentors.  But in the rest of the country, there are many schools that need assistance to enable them “to teach law in a grand manner,” as do Ateneo and Silliman.  The idea is to encourage academic professionals to have a full-time career in teaching law, for which they will be adequately paid — as much as, for example, professors at the Asian Institute of Management.

Also, we know that there are several poor but bright students who need assistance to pursue their legal studies both here and abroad.  The Foundation will assist them in realizing their dreams.

*Second,* the Court has created a Special Committee on Legal Education — chaired by Justice Jose C. Vitug — which is looking into the law school curriculum with a view to making it more responsive to the needs of the new century.  Corollary to this is another group headed by retired Justice Ameurfina Melencio A. Herrera, which aims at reforming the bar examinations to attune them to new requirements.

*Third,* the Court, also through the Centenary Executive Committee, is sponsoring a national moot court competition under a sub-committee headed by former UP Law Dean Merlin M. Magallona.  The competition and judging guidelines have been drawn and may be requested through Atty. Ismael G. Khan Jr., the Supreme Court’s public information officer and the executive officer of its centenary celebrations.  Regional contests will soon be held.  The national finals will be concluded in Manila next year.

*Fourth,* also currently being held under the auspices of the Centenary Executive Committee is a National Speech Festival, involving debates as well as oratorical and essay contests for law students.  Co-sponsored by the Philippine Association of Law Schools, this competition is supervised by a sub-committee headed by former Law Dean and now Justice Secretary Hernando B. Perez, with Dean Ponciano Subido as vice chair.  FEU Dean Andres Bautista is in charge of the essay contest; MLQ Dean Norberto Gonzales, the oratorical contest; and UP Dean Raul Pangalangan, the debates.  Seventy-five law schools from nine regions are participating.

The debate we just witnessed tonight is not an integral part of the National Speech Festival I spoke about.  But the Centenary Executive Committee, which I have the honor of chairing, has decided to make this debate a special feature of our centenary celebrations.  That is why I am here tonight as guest speaker.  That is also why we have decided to support the dual meet with a donation of cash prizes for the winners — the best speaker, the best debater and the best rebuttal speaker — and to subsidize the transportation and lodging stipends for the visiting debaters.

And after watching the debate tonight, I am more than satisfied that we have indeed wisely decided on including it as a special feature of our centenary celebrations.  Truly, the debate was excellent, exciting and worthwhile, a special project we can all be proud of!

The Supreme Court encourages forensic competitions amongst law students.  The most basic requirement for those aspiring to be lawyers is the mastery of language, of effective communication, both

of the written and the spoken varieties.  A student who does not have a singular command of communication skills has no place in the noble profession of law.

Language is a time binder.  It is responsible for the success of humanity and ensures its superiority to all other creatures on this planet.  Humans have built a civilization of prosperity and technological might, because they have been able to communicate their discoveries and wisdom to their successors and heirs through the medium of language.  It took Gregor Mendel a lifetime to discover the law of dominant and recessive characteristics; and Albert Einstein, the theory of relativity.  But because Mendel and Einstein recorded their knowledge in clear, precise language, their discoveries can be explained to and known by the rest of mankind in just a matter of hours.  Indeed, language binds time and enables humans to dominate the beasts of the earth and the forces of nature.

By the same token, through the mastery of forensic and writing skills, men and women of the law are able to discover the truth, prove their legal theories, formulate legal principles and render justice to all.  In no other profession is the mastery of language more critical than in law.

It is in this spirit of binding time and of advancing truth and justice that I congratulate both the Ateneo de Manila and the Silliman University debating teams for their excellent forensic skills.  May you never tire of developing your talent to even higher standards, and may you become the competent and ethical lawyers that you aspire to be.

*Maraming salamat po.*
