**FOREWORD**

	In what has become an unfailing annual ritual, the bench and bar, as well as the academic and business communities, eagerly look forward to the launching of yet another book by Justice Artemio V. Panganiban. “A Centenary of Justice” is the fifth book in a series that chronicles the preceding twelve months of his magistracy on the High Court. This opus could not have been produced at a more appropriate time when, by force of juridical and moral circumstances, the black-robed members of the Court were thrust into center-stage at an extremely crucial point in the history of our Republic whose moorings teetered precariously into collapsing.

	It is an incongruous anomaly that these grave threats to our democratic institutions should be taking place when the Supreme Court was in the midst of celebrations to mark the 100th year of an otherwise humdrum existence – what Justice Panganiban refers to in his book as “A Renaissance in the Judiciary.” As the Chairman of the Executive Committee in charge of commemorating the Court’s Centennial this year, Justice Panganiban applied body and soul to ensure its resounding success. Amazingly, he has also managed to produce approximately 100 *ponencias,* 10 separate opinions and hundreds of minute resolutions in this relatively short span, his sixth year on the Court.

xix

xx

	It will please the reader to know that the twenty chapters comprising Justice Panganiban’s latest *obra maestra* are a pleasurable read, equally interesting, instructive, and easy to understand as any of the decisions, rulings, and resolutions that he scribes for the Court. The first twelve chapters on the “Centenary Celebrations” provide an account of the fifty-odd festivities lauding the achievements of the first 100 years of the Supreme Court in all thirteen judicial regions of the country. The succeeding eight chapters afford the reader an insider’s first-person retelling of the “Judicial Jousts” which take place in the *sanctum sanctorum* of the Highest Court in the land. It is a testimony to the author’s literary and journalistic skills that he can instill in the reader a vicarious involvement in what is ordinarily expected to be prosaic prose. Of contemporaneous interest is Chapter 13 which incisively captures the Court’s dilemma in deciding the legitimacy of Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo’s succession to the presidency. Although there was neither moral nor legal compulsion, it was bittersweet irony that Justice Panganiban and I had to voluntarily inhibit ourselves as participants in this unanimous decision of the Supreme Court, defining a watershed event of truly momentous proportions that would be cast in concrete at the portals of constitutionalism. Be that as it may, I take comfort in the fact – and I surmise that Justice Panganiban does, too – that our recusal has further served to strengthen the independence of the judiciary and the rule of law.

	It is gratifying that Justice Panganiban will still be wearing his judicial robes for more than five years. His readers and admirers are thus reassured that they will continue to have an enviable window to the Supreme Court.

Manila, 15 October 2001.

**PREFACE**

**M**y sixth year as a jurist — October 11, 2000 to October 10, 2001 — was highlighted by the Supreme Court’s observance of its centenary.

	The Supreme Court (SC) reckons its founding on June 11, 1901. On that date, the Second Philippine Commission enacted Act 136, otherwise known as the Judiciary Law. Many, many years earlier, there were entities that were roughly its equivalent in our governmental structure then, like the Royal Audiencia during the Spanish times and the Supreme Court during the American military regime. The Court, however, still considers June 11, 1901 as its birthday, because Act 136 established an *independent* judicial hierarchy, with the Supreme Court as its apex. Prior to that date, its equivalents were hardly more than extensions of the executive department of the government.

	To celebrate this once-in-a-lifetime occasion, the Court formed a Centenary Executive Committee (ExeCom), which I was asked to chair. The celebrations started in June 2000 and culminated on June 11, 2001, the exact 100th birthday of the Court. Thereafter, the celebrations were extended another

	The ExeCom has lined up more than 50 projects and programs of various kinds — intellectual, cultural, historical, educational and physical — involving various sectors of society in the celebrations held throughout the whole country.

	My sixth year in office was thus marked by my work in two general types of activities: first, my usual duties as an ordinary member of the Court; and second, my role as head of our centennial activities. To reflect these two types of work, this book has been divided into two main parts.

	Part I contains various speeches delivered and articles written in connection with the Court’s centenary celebrations. While the book does not purport to be the official report of the ExeCom, it nonetheless discusses various projects and programs held during the celebrations and stresses my personal participation in them. In other words, this volume is not meant to be an authoritative and exhaustive report of the ExeCom; rather, like the previous books I have annually written, it is a report of my work during my sixth year as a member of the Supreme Court.

	Part II, on the other hand, discusses the controversial Decisions of the Court in my sixth year in office, during which I took an active part either as ponente or as a writer of a dissenting or a separate opinion.

	During this period, I accomplished my usual workload and produced over 100 ponencias and 10 separate opinions, thus bringing my grand total at the

After all, decision-making is my primary duty; the centenary celebrations, only my secondary task. 	On page 6 of my last book, *Transparency, Unanimity **&** Diversity,* I mentioned that as of October 10, 2000, the Supreme Court had a backlog of about one thousand cases, compared with about two thousand on the same day in 1998 and six thousand in 1986. Now it is down to a negligible number, perhaps less than fifty. This dramatic reduction was made possible by our centenary “zero backlog” program, under which all cases docketed with GR numbers “120000” or lower were redistributed among the senior justices who had no backlog, and who were thus able to study and promptly decide them. In turn, the more junior members — to whom these low-numbered “old” cases had initially been assigned or “unloaded” — likewise gave them priority. The net result is “no more backlog.”

	But I am not clapping yet. While the “below 120000” cases have almost all been resolved, there are the “below 125000” that are about to breach the twenty-four month constitutional deadline. Indeed, “zero backlog” is a continuing effort.

	Some well-meaning critics say that lawyers tend to write in boring legalese. In an effort to be accurate, objective and emphatic, they tend to use run-on sentences and to be repetitive in their language. Their words often sound pedantic and sometimes fall short on grammar and tenses. They are fond of using therein, thereen and thereat. Instead of the pronoun it, the more legalistic “the same” is employed. In lieu of using only one verb like sell in an agreement, legalists “sell, transfer, convey, cede and assign by way of absolute sale.”⁴

	On the other hand, my friends who regularly read my writings have asked me why I make a deliberate effort to use simple words and simple sentences. My answer is equally simple: I want to be understood not only by lawyers, but also by their clients who read my work. While there are instances in which legalese cannot be avoided, I always strive to write with the hope that even high school graduates would comprehend me.

	In the Old Testament, God laid down only Ten Commandments. In the New Testament, our Lord reduced them to two: Love God first and foremost, and then love your neighbor as you love yourself. But throughout history, humans have complicated these simple commands by promulgating countless constitutions, codes, statutes and rules. It now takes almost a lifetime of patient study to know that we really do not know the law.

	Indeed, human law is awesome in its complexity. The challenge is always how to express it in all simplicity, so that it can be appreciated and obeyed in all sincerity.
