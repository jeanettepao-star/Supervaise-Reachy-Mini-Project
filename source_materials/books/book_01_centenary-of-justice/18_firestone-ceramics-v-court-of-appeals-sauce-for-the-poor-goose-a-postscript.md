# Chapter 18: Firestone Ceramics v. Court of Appeals: — Sauce for the Poor Goose — a Postscript

**C**hapter 22 of my last book — *Transparency, Unanimity **&** Diversity*1 — discussed what I thought (and still think) was the inappropriate elevation of a Decision of the Third Division to the full Court.  It was inappropriate because the movants who lost in that Decision “had utterly failed to adduce any legal reason for such referral.”  However, by a vote of 8 to 7,2 the Court en banc “decided to yank the cases out of and against the will of said Division and treated [them] as en banc cases.”

*Reconsideration*

*Unanimously Overruled*

Let me now write on how the full Court ruled on the Motion for Reconsideration filed by the petitioner-

movants, who had challenged the Third Division’s Decision.  After hearing oral arguments from the parties, the full Court, on January 30, 2001, issued a Resolution3 *unanimously* denying the Motion for Reconsideration and upholding the Third Division’s equally unanimous original Decision, on the following grounds:

1.  “Petitioner’s arguments have been squarely discussed and x x x fully considered in [the Third Division’s] Decision and we reiterate that the Court of Appeals did not err in denying the petition to annul judgment in view of the decision of this Court in the *Margolles* case.”  Explained the Court:

“Petitioner’s contention that it was never a party to the *Margolles* case is not decisive of the issue.  We have stated that absolute identity of parties is not required but only substantial identity of parties for the application of the rule on res judicata.  We agree with the respondent Court of Appeals that although petitioner was not a party in the *Margolles* case, its claim in the instant case and that of the losing parties in the *Margolles* case raised exactly the same argument and relied on the same evidence to justify invalidation of OCT No. 4216[;] namely, that said title supposedly covers unclassified public land (forest land) so that the CFI of Rizal, sitting as a land registration court in 1929, did not acquire jurisdiction to adjudicate the subject property in favor of the original applicants, the Gana spouses.  Such

a shared identity of interest as shown by the identity of the relief sought by one person in a prior case and the second person [in] the subsequent case, i.e., to declare the nullity of Oct No. 4216, is sufficient to make them ‘privy in law’4 for purposes of the operation of the rule on res judicata or conclusiveness of judgment.”

2.  “While it may be true that an applicant for original registration of a parcel of land bears the burden of overcoming the presumption that the land sought to be registered forms part of public domain, compliance with this burden should [have been] proven in the original proceedings instituted by the Gana spouses in 1927, and must [have been] legally presumed to have been satisfied as a decree was issued in favor of the petitioners-spouses.  Thus, where a petition is filed to annul the said judgment on the ground that it is fatally void, the burden of proving the nullity rests with the petitioner.”

3.  “Contrary to the Republic’s posture” in its Motion for Reconsideration, the State was in no danger of being “deprived of its patrimony” by reason of the Third Division’s Decision.  If the Motion is granted and the disputed land is reverted in favor of the government, the parcel recovered “would not [have been devoted] to public use, but [would have been reserved] for eventual disposition to other private persons,” particularly to the private petitioner, Firestone Ceramics, Inc.

3  In this Resolution, Justices Santiago and Gutierrez took “no part.”

4  *Valencia v. RTC of Quezon City,* 184 SCRA 80, April 3, 1990; *Comilang v. Buendia,* 21 SCRA 486, October 25, 1967; *Santos v. Gabriel,* 45 SCRA 288, May 31, 1972; *Widows and Orphans Associations v. CA,* 212 SCRA 360, August 7, 1992.

“Indeed, to annul the existing torrens titles derived from OCT No. 4216 which was issued seventy years ago, only to enable the government to dispose anew the lands covered thereby to new or subsequent applicants would betray a subservience to technicality that will not be in the public interest [or] serve our declared national land policies any useful purpose.  *This is a simple matter of equity and good sense.*”

*Rationale for*

*Inclusion in This Book*

I decided to include the foregoing short discussion in this book to complete and put to rest Chapter 22 of *Transparency.*  I thought that the unanimous Resolution dated January 30, 2001 also showed the folly of failing to heed the maxim “Sauce for the poor goose should be the same sauce for the rich gander.”  As it is, the banc’s inappropriate (in my humble opinion) takeover of *Firestone Ceramics v. Court of Appeals* merely resulted in a waste of precious time and attention; at bottom, it uselessly and imprudently clogged the Court’s dockets.
